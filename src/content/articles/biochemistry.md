# What Is Biochemistry?

Biochemistry is the scientific discipline that studies the chemical structures, reactions, and processes occurring within and between living cells. It sits precisely at the intersection of [biology](/biology) and [chemistry](/chemistry), using the tools and principles of both to explain how the molecules of life — proteins, nucleic acids, lipids, and carbohydrates — drive everything from muscle contraction to memory formation to photosynthesis.

## The Molecules That Make You

Your body is, at its most reductive level, a collection of chemical reactions. About 37 trillion cells, each containing billions of molecules, all interacting according to the same chemical principles that govern reactions in a test tube. The difference is complexity — and that complexity is exactly what biochemistry tries to untangle.

Four major classes of biomolecules make up virtually all living matter. Understanding these four is understanding biochemistry's core subject.

### Proteins: The Workers

Proteins do almost everything. They catalyze reactions (enzymes), provide structural support (collagen, keratin), transport molecules (hemoglobin carries oxygen), transmit signals (insulin), fight infections (antibodies), and generate movement (actin and myosin in muscles). If your body were a city, proteins would be the workers, the buildings, the delivery trucks, and the police force.

Every protein is a chain of amino acids — small molecules linked together by peptide bonds. There are 20 standard amino acids, and the specific sequence in which they're linked determines the protein's identity and function. Human cells produce roughly 20,000 different proteins, each with a unique amino acid sequence encoded in DNA.

Here's where it gets interesting. A protein's function depends not just on its sequence but on its three-dimensional shape. The amino acid chain folds into specific structures — alpha helices, beta sheets, loops — driven by hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and other forces. This folding happens spontaneously, usually within milliseconds to seconds, and the resulting shape creates the active sites, binding pockets, and structural features that give the protein its function.

When proteins misfold, bad things happen. Prion diseases (like mad cow disease) involve proteins that adopt an incorrect fold and then force neighboring proteins to misfold too — a molecular chain reaction. Alzheimer's disease involves the accumulation of misfolded amyloid-beta proteins. Understanding protein folding is one of the biggest challenges in biochemistry. DeepMind's AlphaFold, an AI system that predicts protein structures from amino acid sequences, represented a massive breakthrough when it was released in 2021 — solving a problem that had stumped biochemists for 50 years.

### Nucleic Acids: The Information

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and RNA (ribonucleic acid) store and transmit genetic information. The structure of DNA — the famous double helix discovered by Watson and Crick in 1953, with critical [X-ray crystallography](/x-ray-crystallography) data from Rosalind Franklin — is one of the most important findings in all of science.

DNA consists of two strands of nucleotides wound around each other. Each nucleotide contains a sugar (deoxyribose), a phosphate group, and one of four bases: adenine (A), thymine (T), guanine (G), and cytosine (C). The bases pair specifically — A with T, G with C — through hydrogen bonds. This base pairing is what makes DNA replication possible: unzip the strands, and each one acts as a template for building a complementary copy.

The human genome contains approximately 3.2 billion base pairs encoding about 20,000-25,000 protein-coding genes. But here's a fact that surprised even geneticists: only about 1.5% of the genome codes for proteins. The rest — once dismissed as "junk DNA" — includes regulatory sequences, structural elements, transposable elements, and large stretches whose functions are still being discovered.

RNA serves multiple roles. Messenger RNA (mRNA) carries genetic instructions from DNA to ribosomes. Transfer RNA (tRNA) delivers amino acids during protein synthesis. Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) forms the structural and catalytic core of ribosomes. And non-coding RNAs — microRNAs, long non-coding RNAs, and others — regulate gene expression in ways that were barely understood 20 years ago and are now recognized as enormously important.

### Lipids: The Barriers and Signals

Lipids are a chemically diverse group united by one property: they don't dissolve in water. This makes them perfect for building cell membranes — the barriers that define cells and their internal compartments. Cell membranes are constructed from phospholipid bilayers: two layers of phospholipid molecules arranged with their water-fearing (hydrophobic) tails facing inward and their water-loving (hydrophilic) heads facing outward.

Beyond membranes, lipids serve as energy storage (fats store about 9 calories per gram, more than twice the energy density of carbohydrates or proteins), signaling molecules (steroid hormones like testosterone and estrogen are lipids), and insulation (the myelin sheath around nerve fibers is lipid-rich, enabling fast signal transmission).

Cholesterol, often villainized in health discussions, is actually essential. It's a component of every animal cell membrane, a precursor to steroid hormones and vitamin D, and required for bile acid synthesis. The health problems associated with cholesterol relate to its transport in the blood — specifically, the balance between LDL (which delivers cholesterol to tissues) and HDL (which removes it).

### Carbohydrates: The Fuel and Framework

Carbohydrates range from simple sugars (glucose, fructose) to complex polysaccharides (starch, cellulose, glycogen). Glucose is the primary fuel for most cells — it's broken down through glycolysis and the citric acid cycle to produce ATP, the universal energy currency of life.

But carbohydrates do far more than provide energy. Cellulose — a polymer of glucose — is the most abundant organic molecule on Earth, forming the structural framework of plant cell walls. Chitin, another glucose-related polymer, builds the exoskeletons of insects and crustaceans. Glycoproteins (proteins with attached sugar chains) are critical for cell recognition, immune function, and blood typing.

## Metabolism: The Chemistry of Staying Alive

Metabolism is the total set of chemical reactions occurring in a living organism. It has two halves:

**Catabolism** breaks down molecules to release energy. When you eat food, catabolic pathways disassemble carbohydrates, fats, and proteins into smaller molecules, capturing energy as ATP. The major catabolic pathways — glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and oxidative phosphorylation — are astonishingly well-conserved across life. The same basic process that a bacterium uses to extract energy from glucose is essentially the same one your mitochondria use.

**Anabolism** uses energy to build complex molecules from simpler ones. Protein synthesis, DNA replication, and fat storage are all anabolic processes. They require ATP — which is why catabolism and anabolism are coupled.

### ATP: The Universal Currency

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) deserves special attention because it drives essentially every energy-requiring process in your body. When ATP loses a phosphate group (becoming ADP), energy is released that powers muscle contraction, nerve impulse transmission, protein synthesis, and thousands of other processes.

Your body contains only about 250 grams of ATP at any given moment — roughly enough to sustain life for a few seconds. But you recycle it constantly, regenerating ATP from ADP through oxidative phosphorylation. Over the course of a day, you produce and consume roughly your own body weight in ATP. That's about 70 kg of ATP generated and used every 24 hours. The turnover is staggering.

### The Citric Acid Cycle

Also called the Krebs cycle (after Hans Krebs, who won the 1953 Nobel Prize for working it out), this circular pathway sits at the metabolic crossroads. It takes acetyl-CoA — derived from carbohydrates, fats, and proteins — and oxidizes it, producing CO2, a small amount of ATP, and (most importantly) electron carriers (NADH and FADH2) that feed into the electron transport chain.

The citric acid cycle doesn't just generate energy. Its intermediates are starting materials for amino acid synthesis, fatty acid production, and nucleotide biosynthesis. It connects every major metabolic pathway, which is why understanding it is central to understanding biochemistry.

### Oxidative Phosphorylation

This is where the big ATP payoff happens. NADH and FADH2 from the citric acid cycle deliver electrons to a series of protein complexes embedded in the inner mitochondrial membrane. As electrons pass along this chain, energy is used to pump protons (H+ ions) across the membrane, creating an electrochemical gradient — essentially a biological battery. Protons flow back through ATP synthase (a remarkable molecular motor that literally spins as protons pass through it), driving the synthesis of ATP from ADP.

This process generates roughly 30-32 ATP molecules per glucose molecule — vastly more than the 2 ATP produced by glycolysis alone. It's the reason aerobic organisms (like us) extract so much more energy from food than anaerobic organisms.

## Enzymes: Biology's Catalysts

Enzymes are proteins that accelerate specific chemical reactions by factors of 10^6 to 10^17. Without enzymes, the reactions necessary for life would occur too slowly to sustain a living organism. Decomposing hydrogen peroxide, for instance, happens spontaneously but very slowly. Catalase — an enzyme in your red blood cells — speeds this reaction by a factor of 10^7, converting hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen almost instantly.

### How Enzymes Work

The lock-and-key model (proposed by Emil Fischer in 1894) suggests that an enzyme's active site has a fixed shape complementary to its substrate. The induced-fit model (Daniel Koshland, 1958) is more accurate: the active site changes shape slightly upon substrate binding, optimizing the interaction. This conformational change helps position substrate molecules for the reaction and can strain chemical bonds, lowering the activation energy.

Enzyme kinetics — the study of reaction rates — was formalized by Leonor Michaelis and Maud Menten in 1913. Their equation describes how reaction rate depends on substrate concentration, with two key parameters: Vmax (maximum rate when the enzyme is saturated) and Km (the substrate concentration at half Vmax). Km is a rough measure of how tightly an enzyme binds its substrate.

### Regulation

Cells must precisely control when and how fast each reaction occurs. Enzyme regulation mechanisms include:

**Allosteric regulation** — molecules bind to sites other than the active site, changing the enzyme's shape and activity. This allows feedback loops: the end product of a metabolic pathway can inhibit an early enzyme in the same pathway, preventing overproduction.

**Covalent modification** — adding or removing chemical groups (particularly phosphate groups, in a process called phosphorylation) can switch enzymes on or off. Protein kinases add phosphate groups; protein phosphatases remove them. This phosphorylation/dephosphorylation cycle is the primary mechanism for signal transduction inside cells.

**Gene regulation** — cells can increase or decrease the production of specific enzymes by turning genes on or off. This is a slower response (minutes to hours) compared to allosteric regulation (milliseconds) but allows more fundamental changes in metabolic capacity.

## Biochemistry and Medicine

Modern medicine is biochemistry applied. Nearly every drug targets a specific molecular pathway, and understanding those pathways requires biochemical knowledge.

**Drug design** increasingly uses structural biochemistry — knowing the three-dimensional shape of a target protein (often determined by X-ray [crystallography](/crystallography) or cryo-[electron microscopy](/electron-microscopy)) to design molecules that fit into its active site or binding pocket. The HIV protease inhibitors that transformed AIDS from a death sentence to a manageable chronic condition were designed this way.

**Diagnostic testing** is almost entirely biochemical. Blood glucose monitoring measures the concentration of a small molecule. Pregnancy tests detect the hormone hCG using antibodies. PCR (polymerase chain reaction) amplifies DNA sequences for pathogen detection, forensic identification, and genetic testing. CRISPR-based diagnostics — like the SHERLOCK system developed during COVID-19 — use enzymatic reactions to detect specific nucleic acid sequences.

**Cancer biology** is fundamentally biochemistry. Cancers result from mutations in genes that control cell growth and division — oncogenes and tumor suppressors. Targeted cancer therapies (like imatinib for chronic myeloid leukemia, which specifically inhibits the BCR-ABL fusion protein) represent biochemistry translated directly into treatment.

## The Techniques of Modern Biochemistry

Several analytical methods define the field:

**[Chromatography](/chromatography)** separates molecules based on their physical properties — size, charge, hydrophobicity, or specific binding interactions. Column chromatography can purify a single protein from a mixture containing thousands.

**Electrophoresis** separates molecules (particularly proteins and nucleic acids) by size and charge in an electric field. SDS-PAGE (for proteins) and agarose gel electrophoresis (for DNA) are everyday [laboratory](/biology) techniques.

**Mass spectrometry** identifies molecules by their mass-to-charge ratio. [Proteomics](/proteomics) — the large-scale study of all proteins in a cell — relies heavily on mass spectrometry to identify and quantify thousands of proteins simultaneously.

**X-ray crystallography** determines the atomic structure of molecules by analyzing how X-rays diffract through crystals. It has produced the structures of tens of thousands of proteins, including many drug targets. Cryo-electron [microscopy](/microscopy) (cryo-EM) has emerged as a complementary technique, particularly for large molecular complexes that are difficult to crystallize.

**[Spectroscopy](/spectroscopy)** — UV-visible, fluorescence, circular dichroism, NMR — provides information about molecular concentration, structure, and dynamics. These are the workhorse measurements of daily biochemistry research.

## The Frontiers

Biochemistry isn't a solved field — far from it. Several areas are advancing rapidly:

**[Synthetic biology](/synthetic-biology)** applies [engineering](/engineering) principles to biological systems, designing new [metabolic pathways](/metabolic-pathways), creating organisms with novel capabilities, and building biological circuits. Engineering bacteria to produce biofuels, pharmaceuticals, or industrial chemicals is a growing application that draws directly on biochemical understanding of metabolism.

**Metabolomics** — the thorough study of all small molecules (metabolites) in a cell or organism — is the newest "omics" field. By measuring thousands of metabolites simultaneously, researchers can detect metabolic signatures of diseases, identify drug targets, and understand how diet and environment affect health at the molecular level.

**[Structural biology](/structural-biology) at atomic resolution** continues to advance. AlphaFold and similar AI systems have predicted structures for essentially every known protein sequence, creating a structural database that would have taken experimental methods decades to build. But predicted structures aren't perfect — experimental validation remains essential, and understanding protein dynamics (how structures change over time) is the next frontier.

**Epigenetics** — chemical modifications to DNA and its associated proteins that affect gene expression without changing the DNA sequence — has revealed a layer of biochemical regulation that was largely invisible 30 years ago. DNA methylation, histone modification, and chromatin remodeling control which genes are active in which cells, and disruptions in epigenetic regulation are implicated in cancer, aging, and developmental disorders.

The field keeps expanding because life keeps revealing new layers of chemical complexity. Every time biochemists think they've mapped a complete pathway, they discover regulatory mechanisms, feedback loops, or cross-talk with other pathways that they hadn't suspected. It's frustrating and exhilarating in roughly equal measure — the molecular machinery of life is both more elegant and more tangled than anyone imagined.
