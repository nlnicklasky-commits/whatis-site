# What Is Candy Making?

Candy making ([confectionery](/confectionery)) is the craft of creating sweet treats by manipulating sugar — heating it to precise temperatures, combining it with other ingredients, and shaping the results into everything from soft fudge to hard lollipops to silky truffles. It's one of the most temperature-sensitive forms of [cooking](/cooking), where a few degrees can mean the difference between success and a sticky disaster.

## Sugar Science

The [chemistry](/food-[chemistry](/chemistry)) of candy making is fundamentally about sugar and water. When you dissolve sugar in water and heat the solution, water evaporates. The higher the temperature, the less water remains, and the harder the cooled candy will be. This is why candy recipes specify exact temperatures rather than cooking times.

The stages, from lowest to highest temperature:

- **Thread (230-235°F)** — Syrup forms threads when dripped. Used for glazes.
- **Soft ball (235-240°F)** — A drop of syrup forms a soft, pliable ball in cold water. Fudge, pralines.
- **Firm ball (245-250°F)** — Firmer ball. Caramels.
- **Hard ball (250-265°F)** — Hard but still pliable ball. Nougat, marshmallows, rock candy.
- **Soft crack (270-290°F)** — Syrup stretches and bends before breaking. Taffy, butterscotch.
- **Hard crack (300-310°F)** — Syrup snaps cleanly. Lollipops, toffee, peanut brittle.
- **Caramel (320-350°F)** — Sugar breaks down and develops complex flavor and brown color.

A candy thermometer is non-negotiable. Digital probe thermometers with high-temperature range provide the accuracy needed. The traditional "cold water test" — dropping syrup into ice water and observing its behavior — works but requires experience to judge correctly.

## Major Candy Categories

### Crystalline Candies

Fudge, fondant, and rock candy are crystalline — they contain sugar crystals that determine texture. In fudge, tiny, uniform crystals produce smooth creaminess. Large crystals produce grainy, unpleasant texture.

Controlling crystallization is the central challenge. Factors include: avoiding disturbance during cooling (stirring too early creates large crystals), adding interfering agents (corn syrup, cream of tartar, butter — each prevents sugar molecules from organizing into large crystals), and cooling temperature before beating.

### Non-Crystalline (Amorphous) Candies

Hard candy, caramels, taffy, and toffee are amorphous — the sugar cools without forming crystals, creating glassy or chewy textures. These candies are cooked to higher temperatures, removing more water and producing harder results.

Caramels are particularly interesting — they combine sugar's sweetness with the Maillard reaction (sugar reacting with milk proteins at high temperatures) to produce complex butterscotch and toffee flavors. The ratio of sugar, butter, and cream, combined with final temperature, determines whether you get soft, chewy caramels or hard toffee.

### Chocolate

Chocolate confections — truffles, bonbons, bars, bark — involve working with tempered chocolate. Tempering controls cocoa butter crystallization to produce the snap, sheen, and melt-in-your-mouth quality of professional chocolate.

The process is fussy but learnable: melt chocolate to 115-120°F (dark), cool to 79-82°F while stirring constantly, then gently rewarm to 87-90°F. The result is chocolate with a smooth, glossy finish that snaps cleanly — a quality called "temper" that distinguishes professional work from amateur.

### Gummy and Jelly Candies

Gummies use gelatin, pectin, or starch to create chewy textures. The setting agent, sugar concentration, acid level (citric acid provides the sour coating), and flavoring combine to produce everything from gummy bears to Turkish delight to French pâtes de fruit.

## Home Candy Making

The pandemic-era cooking trend extended to candy making, with searches for fudge, caramel, and chocolate truffle recipes spiking dramatically. Home candy making is genuinely accessible — most recipes use common ingredients and basic equipment.

Good beginner projects include:
- **Fudge** — Requires careful temperature control but simple ingredients
- **Peanut brittle** — Hard crack stage, dramatic to make, impressive results
- **Chocolate truffles** — No cooking required; just ganache (chocolate + cream) rolled in cocoa
- **Caramels** — More challenging but extremely rewarding

The investment is minimal: a heavy saucepan, a candy thermometer, parchment paper, and ingredients you probably already have. The learning curve is real — sugar is unforgiving of inattention — but the rewards come quickly.

## The Confectionery Industry

The U.S. confectionery industry generates over $37 billion annually. Major manufacturers (Mars, Hershey, Mondelez) produce familiar brands at enormous scale, while artisan chocolatiers and candy makers operate thousands of small shops producing handmade confections.

The craft chocolate movement — small producers making chocolate directly from cacao beans rather than buying pre-made chocolate — has grown significantly since the early 2000s. Bean-to-bar makers emphasize cacao origin, minimal ingredients, and flavor complexity, much like craft [beer brewers](/beer-brewing) emphasize ingredients and process.

Candy making is chemistry you can eat, art you can share, and a tradition spanning thousands of years. The Egyptians made candy from honey and fruit. Medieval Europeans created marzipan and sugar sculptures. Modern confectioners work with the same fundamental science — sugar, heat, and time — that candy makers have always used. The tools are better; the sweet tooth hasn't changed at all.
