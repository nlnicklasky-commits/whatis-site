# What Is Confectionery?

Confectionery is the craft of making sweets — candies, chocolates, caramels, marshmallows, fudge, and everything else that satisfies a sugar craving. It's also the industry that produces these products, a global market worth over $230 billion annually. Behind every gummy bear and truffle lies a surprising amount of [chemistry](/chemistry), technique, and centuries of culinary tradition.

## Sugar Science

The foundation of almost all confectionery is sugar — specifically, understanding how sugar behaves at different temperatures. This is where [candy making](/candy-making) becomes genuinely scientific.

When you dissolve sugar in water and heat the solution, water evaporates and the sugar concentration increases. The temperature of the syrup tells you exactly what the sugar is doing, and each temperature range produces a different candy texture:

- **Thread stage (230-235°F / 110-113°C):** Sugar syrup spins into threads. Used for spun sugar decorations.
- **Soft ball (235-240°F / 113-116°C):** The syrup forms a soft, pliable ball in cold water. Used for fudge and pralines.
- **Firm ball (245-250°F / 118-121°C):** Firmer ball that holds shape. Used for caramels.
- **Hard ball (250-266°F / 121-130°C):** Rigid but still slightly pliable. Used for nougat and marshmallows.
- **Soft crack (270-290°F / 132-143°C):** Threads that bend before breaking. Used for taffy and butterscotch.
- **Hard crack (300-310°F / 149-154°C):** Threads that snap cleanly. Used for lollipops, toffee, and hard candy.

Beyond hard crack, sugar caramelizes — the molecules break down and recombine into hundreds of new compounds that produce the complex, bittersweet flavor we call caramel. Push further and you get burnt sugar. The window between perfect caramel and burnt mess is narrow, sometimes just 10-15 degrees.

A candy thermometer is the confectioner's most important tool. The difference between fudge and toffee is essentially a temperature difference of about 70 degrees Fahrenheit.

## The Two Kingdoms

Confectionery divides into two broad categories:

**Sugar confectionery** includes everything made primarily from sugar: hard candies, gummies, marshmallows, licorice, caramels, toffees, and fondant. Each type involves manipulating sugar at specific temperatures and adding ingredients that modify texture — gelatin for gummies, corn syrup to prevent crystallization in hard candies, egg whites for marshmallows.

**Chocolate confectionery** involves products made with or coated in chocolate: truffles, pralines, bonbons, chocolate bars, and enrobed (chocolate-covered) items. Chocolate work requires understanding tempering — the process of carefully heating and cooling chocolate to produce the right crystal structure in the cocoa butter.

Properly tempered chocolate has a glossy finish and a clean snap when broken. The science behind it involves polymorphic crystallization — cocoa butter can form six different crystal types, but only Type V (beta crystals, forming at about 34°C/93°F) produces the desirable qualities. Miss the temperature window and you get dull, crumbly, or "bloomed" (white-streaked) chocolate.

## From Ancient Honey to Industrial Production

The earliest confections were simple combinations of honey, fruits, nuts, and spices. Ancient Egyptians, Greeks, and Romans all made sweet treats, though sugar as we know it didn't reach Europe until the Crusades brought it from the Middle East in the 11th century.

For centuries, sugar was a luxury — literally as expensive as spices. Confections were reserved for royalty and the wealthy. Apothecaries sold sugar-coated medicines, and the line between medicine and candy was genuinely blurry until the 18th century.

The industrial revolution changed everything. Mechanized sugar refining dropped prices. New machinery could pull taffy, mold chocolates, and wrap candies at unprecedented scale. By the late 1800s, companies like Cadbury, Nestle, and Hershey were mass-producing affordable chocolate. The candy bar — arguably the confection that defined the 20th century — emerged in this era.

## The Modern Industry

The global confectionery industry produces roughly 25 million metric tons of product annually. Mars, Mondelez International, Ferrero, Nestle, and Hershey dominate the market, but thousands of smaller artisan producers thrive in the premium segment.

The industry divides roughly into:
- **Chocolate products** (45% of global [sales](/sales))
- **Sugar candy** (35%)
- **Gum** (20%)

Seasonal sales drive enormous volume. Halloween is the biggest candy-buying occasion in the U.S. ($4.6 billion in 2023), followed by Easter, Valentine's Day, and Christmas. Americans consume roughly 25 pounds of candy per person annually — less than some European countries, where per-capita consumption can exceed 30 pounds.

## The Artisan Revival

While industrial confectionery dominates by volume, the artisan and craft segment has grown significantly. Bean-to-bar chocolate makers — who control the entire process from raw cacao to finished bar — have multiplied from a handful in 2005 to over 500 worldwide.

Artisan confectioners emphasize quality ingredients, small-batch production, and distinctive flavors. A mass-market chocolate bar uses 8-12% cacao. A craft dark chocolate bar might use 70-85% single-origin cacao, and the flavor difference is dramatic.

This parallels trends in [craft beer](/craft-beer), specialty coffee, and artisan [baking](/baking) — consumers willing to pay more for products made with care and transparency. The market supports both a $1 candy bar and a $12 artisan chocolate tablet, serving different needs at different moments.

## Making Sweets at Home

Home candy making requires minimal equipment — a heavy saucepan, a candy thermometer, and patience. Start with simple recipes: fudge (soft ball stage, forgiving of errors), caramels (firm ball, requires attention but not advanced technique), or bark (melted chocolate poured over toppings, barely a recipe at all).

The learning curve steepens with hard candy, pulled taffy, and tempering chocolate. These require precise temperature control and quick handling. But even these are learnable — people made candy at home for centuries before confectionery schools existed.

The satisfaction of producing something genuinely delicious from sugar, butter, and heat is hard to beat. And frankly, a batch of homemade caramels makes a better gift than almost anything you can buy.
