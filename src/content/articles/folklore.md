# What Is Folklore?

Folklore is the collective body of traditions, beliefs, stories, customs, and creative expressions that are passed down within a community — usually orally or through practice rather than through books or formal education. It includes fairy tales, myths, legends, jokes, proverbs, superstitions, folk songs, folk dances, folk art, traditional remedies, and hundreds of other forms. If a community shares it, and it's transmitted informally, it's probably folklore.

## Broader Than You Think

Most people hear "folklore" and think of old fairy tales and myths. That's a sliver of the actual field. Folklore encompasses:

**Verbal lore** — stories, jokes, proverbs, riddles, tongue twisters, insults, blessings, curses, and speechmaking traditions. That joke your uncle tells at every Thanksgiving? Folklore.

**Material lore** — folk art, folk architecture, traditional food, traditional clothing, quilting patterns, pottery styles. The way your grandmother makes her pie crust — the specific technique passed from her grandmother — is material folklore.

**Customary lore** — rituals, festivals, games, gestures, superstitions, calendar customs. Knocking on wood to avoid bad luck, throwing rice at weddings, blowing out birthday candles and making a wish — all folklore.

**Performative lore** — folk music, folk dance, folk drama, storytelling traditions. When kids play "Ring Around the Rosie" (a rhyme possibly dating to the 1880s or earlier), they're performing folklore.

The word "folklore" was coined in 1846 by English scholar William Thoms as a replacement for "popular antiquities." He wanted a single term for the traditional knowledge and practices of ordinary people.

## Categories of Folk Narrative

Stories are folklore's most prominent form, and they come in several recognized types.

**Myths** are sacred narratives about gods, creation, and the fundamental nature of reality. Myths were (or are) believed to be true by the cultures that tell them. Greek myths about Zeus, Hindu stories of Vishnu, Norse tales of Odin — these aren't entertainment. They're explanatory frameworks for how the world works.

**Legends** are stories set in the recognizable world and told as if they happened — but without the sacred character of myths. King Arthur, Robin Hood, Johnny Appleseed. Legends often contain a kernel of historical truth wrapped in elaboration and invention.

**Fairy tales** (or wonder tales) feature magical elements in a generic setting — "once upon a time, in a land far away." They're not believed to be true and aren't set in specific historical contexts. Cinderella, Hansel and Gretel, Jack and the Beanstalk.

**Urban legends** are contemporary legends — stories told as true, set in modern contexts, and spread through informal channels. The alligators in the sewer, the vanishing hitchhiker, the razor blade in the Halloween candy. They express modern anxieties the way older legends expressed earlier ones.

**Personal experience narratives** are true (or believed-true) stories individuals tell about their own lives — ghost encounters, miracle healings, near-death experiences. These personal stories follow recognizable patterns and formulas, making them folklore even when they describe genuine experiences.

## How Folklore Works

Folklore spreads through what scholars call "tradition" — informal, person-to-person transmission. Unlike books or broadcasts, which have fixed forms, folklore varies with each telling. This variation is a feature, not a bug. It's how folklore adapts to new contexts, communities, and times.

The same basic story might exist in hundreds of versions. The Cinderella story type (identified as ATU 510A in the Aarne-Thompson-Uther folk tale index) has been documented in over 700 versions from cultures worldwide. Each reflects local values, social structures, and aesthetic preferences.

Folklore also follows recognizable patterns. Vladimir Propp identified 31 narrative functions in Russian fairy tales. Alan Dundes analyzed the structure of folk beliefs and customs. These patterns persist because they address recurring human situations — growing up, facing danger, seeking justice, coping with death.

## Why Folklore Matters

Folklore does serious cultural work, even when it looks like simple entertainment.

**Cultural transmission.** Folklore teaches values, norms, and practical knowledge informally. Proverbs encode wisdom. Cautionary tales warn against dangerous behavior. Origin stories establish cultural identity. Children absorb their culture's values through folklore before they can read.

**Social cohesion.** Shared stories, customs, and traditions bind communities together. When a family tells the same stories at holiday gatherings, or a community performs the same dances at festivals, they're reinforcing their identity as a group.

**Psychological processing.** Fairy tales help children process fears (abandonment, danger, death) in symbolic form. Urban legends express collective anxieties about technology, crime, or contamination. Ghost stories grapple with mortality. Folklore provides safe containers for difficult emotions.

**Historical record.** While folklore isn't history (legends aren't literal truth), it often preserves memory of real events, social conditions, and cultural contacts. Ballads may record historical events. Customs may preserve traces of earlier beliefs. Place names embedded in legends sometimes correspond to real archaeological features.

## Modern Folklore

Folklore didn't stop when the internet arrived — it accelerated. The internet is the most efficient folklore transmission system ever created.

Memes are visual folklore — images and phrases repeated and varied by millions of participants. Creepypasta (horror stories shared online) functions exactly like traditional ghost stories and legends. Viral challenges are contemporary folk games. Fan fiction is a form of communal storytelling.

The "Slender Man" phenomenon is a textbook example. Created by Eric Knudsen in 2009 as an entry in a Photoshop contest, the character escaped its creator's control immediately. Thousands of people contributed stories, images, and lore, and the character became genuine folklore — collectively owned, endlessly varied, and more powerful than any individual contribution.

Contemporary folklorists study these phenomena alongside traditional forms, recognizing that the process of folklore creation hasn't changed. Only the medium has.

## The Living Tradition

Here's what's easy to miss about folklore: it's not dead. It's not a museum exhibit of quaint old stories. Folklore is something you do every day — every joke you retell, every superstition you follow, every family tradition you maintain, every viral story you share. You are folklore's carrier, editor, and co-creator, whether you recognize it or not.
