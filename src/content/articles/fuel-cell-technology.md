# What Is Fuel Cell Technology?

A fuel cell is an electrochemical device that converts chemical energy directly into electrical energy by combining hydrogen and oxygen, producing water and heat as the only byproducts. Unlike batteries, which store energy and must be recharged, fuel cells generate [electricity](/electricity) continuously as long as fuel is supplied. First demonstrated by Sir William Grove in 1839, fuel cells have powered spacecraft since the 1960s and are now entering commercial markets for transportation, stationary power, and portable [electronics](/electronics).

## How a Fuel Cell Actually Works

The basic principle is elegant, almost deceptively simple. And it starts with something you probably learned in high school chemistry — just running it backward.

Electrolysis uses electricity to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. A fuel cell reverses that process: combine hydrogen and oxygen, and you get water plus electricity. No combustion. No moving parts (in the cell itself). No emissions beyond pure water vapor.

Here's what happens inside a single fuel cell:

**Step 1 — The Anode Side**: Hydrogen gas enters the fuel cell and reaches the anode (the negative electrode). A catalyst — usually platinum — strips the electrons from the hydrogen atoms, splitting each H2 molecule into two hydrogen ions (protons) and two electrons.

**Step 2 — The Membrane**: The proton exchange membrane (in PEM fuel cells) allows the positively charged hydrogen ions to pass through but blocks the electrons. This separation is the key — it forces the electrons to take a different path.

**Step 3 — The External Circuit**: The electrons, blocked by the membrane, flow through an external circuit — and that flow of electrons is electricity. This is the usable power output of the fuel cell. Connect a motor, a light, a computer — the electrons do work on their way to the other side.

**Step 4 — The Cathode Side**: On the other side (the cathode), oxygen from the air meets the arriving hydrogen ions and returning electrons. They combine in a [chemistry](/chemistry) reaction catalyzed by more platinum: 2H+ + 2e- + ½O2 → H2O. Water. That's it.

A single fuel cell produces about 0.6-0.7 volts — not much. So cells are stacked together (a "fuel cell stack") to produce useful voltage and power. A typical automotive fuel cell stack might contain 300-400 individual cells generating 80-120 kilowatts of power.

## Types of Fuel Cells

Not all fuel cells are alike. Different types use different electrolytes, operate at different temperatures, and suit different applications. Here's the field.

### Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) Fuel Cells

PEM cells use a thin polymer membrane as the electrolyte. They operate at relatively low temperatures (60-80°C), start up quickly, and produce high power density relative to their size and weight. These are the fuel cells in hydrogen cars, buses, and forklifts.

**Advantages**: Quick startup, good power density, responsive to changing loads, relatively compact.

**Disadvantages**: Require pure hydrogen (even small amounts of carbon monoxide poison the platinum catalyst), expensive platinum catalysts, need [water management](/water-management) to keep the membrane hydrated.

Toyota's Mirai, Hyundai's NEXO, and Honda's Clarity all use PEM fuel cells. Most fuel cell buses and heavy trucks also use this technology.

### Solid Oxide Fuel Cells (SOFCs)

SOFCs use a solid ceramic electrolyte and operate at extremely high temperatures — 600 to 1,000°C. At these temperatures, the ceramic becomes an oxygen ion conductor.

**Advantages**: Can run on multiple fuels (natural gas, biogas, even diesel) because internal reforming is possible at high temperatures. Very high efficiency, especially in combined heat and power applications (up to 85%). No precious metal catalysts needed.

**Disadvantages**: Slow startup (hours, not minutes), brittle ceramic materials, thermal cycling can cause cracks. Not suitable for vehicles — they're stationary power generators.

Bloom Energy boxes (those flat green units you might see at data centers and commercial buildings) are solid oxide fuel cells running on natural gas. They produce power at the point of use, avoiding transmission losses.

### Alkaline Fuel Cells (AFCs)

The original fuel cell type, AFCs powered the Apollo spacecraft and the Space Shuttle. They use a potassium hydroxide solution as the electrolyte.

**Advantages**: High efficiency, can use non-precious metal catalysts (nickel, silver).

**Disadvantages**: Extremely sensitive to CO2 — even the small amount in regular air degrades performance. This limits them to applications with purified oxygen, making them impractical for most commercial uses.

### Phosphoric Acid Fuel Cells (PAFCs)

PAFCs use liquid phosphoric acid as the electrolyte and operate at around 150-200°C. They were the first commercial fuel cell type, used primarily for stationary power generation.

**Advantages**: Tolerant of impurities in hydrogen, relatively mature technology, long operating life.

**Disadvantages**: Lower efficiency than other types, large and heavy, expensive. Being displaced by PEM and SOFC designs in most applications.

### Molten Carbonate Fuel Cells (MCFCs)

MCFCs operate at about 650°C using a molten lithium-potassium carbonate electrolyte. Like SOFCs, their high temperature allows internal fuel reforming.

**Advantages**: Can use natural gas or biogas directly, high efficiency, no precious metals.

**Disadvantages**: The corrosive molten carbonate electrolyte degrades cell components over time, limiting lifespan.

## The Hydrogen Question

Here's the uncomfortable truth that fuel cell advocates sometimes gloss over: the fuel cell itself is remarkably clean, but the hydrogen fuel might not be.

### Colors of Hydrogen

The industry uses a color-coding system that tells you how the hydrogen was produced:

**Gray hydrogen** (95% of current production): Made by steam methane reforming — heating natural gas with steam to produce hydrogen and CO2. For every kilogram of hydrogen produced, about 10 kg of CO2 are released. This is the cheapest method ($1-2/kg) but defeats the environmental purpose.

**Blue hydrogen**: Same process as gray, but with carbon capture and storage (CCS) to trap the CO2 emissions. Costs $2-3/kg. The effectiveness depends on how much CO2 is actually captured — estimates range from 60% to 90%.

**Green hydrogen**: Produced by electrolysis — splitting water using electricity from renewable sources (solar, wind, hydro). Zero carbon emissions. Currently costs $4-8/kg, but prices are falling rapidly as electrolyzer technology improves and [alternative energy](/alternative-energy) becomes cheaper. Most projections expect green hydrogen to reach $2/kg by 2030 in favorable locations.

**Pink hydrogen**: Electrolysis powered by nuclear energy. Low carbon, relatively constant production.

The pathway to a genuinely clean hydrogen economy requires green (or pink) hydrogen to become cost-competitive with gray. That transition is underway but not yet complete. In the meantime, most hydrogen used in fuel cells comes from natural gas, which reduces but doesn't eliminate the carbon footprint compared to gasoline.

### The Efficiency Debate

Fuel cell skeptics make a valid point about round-trip efficiency. If you start with renewable electricity:

- **Battery electric path**: Electricity → charge battery → discharge battery → motor. Overall efficiency: about 70-80%.
- **Hydrogen fuel cell path**: Electricity → electrolyzer → compress/transport hydrogen → fuel cell → motor. Overall efficiency: about 25-35%.

The hydrogen path loses energy at every conversion step. You need roughly three times as much renewable electricity to drive the same distance on hydrogen versus battery power.

So why bother with hydrogen at all?

Because batteries have limits. They're heavy, they take time to charge, and scaling them up for heavy transport (ships, long-haul trucks, aircraft) is extremely difficult. A fully loaded electric semi-truck would need a battery weighing 15-20 tons — impractical when you're trying to carry cargo. A hydrogen fuel cell system weighs a fraction of that and refuels in minutes.

The efficiency argument is important for passenger cars (where batteries make more sense for most use cases). For heavy transport, industrial heat, seasonal energy storage, and off-grid power, the flexibility of hydrogen outweighs the efficiency penalty.

## Real-World Applications

### Transportation

**Cars**: Toyota, Hyundai, and BMW produce hydrogen fuel cell vehicles. The Toyota Mirai offers about 400 miles of range and refuels in under five minutes. But the infrastructure challenge is severe — there are fewer than 100 public hydrogen stations in the U.S. (most in California) compared to over 60,000 EV charging stations. This chicken-and-egg problem (no cars without stations, no stations without cars) is the biggest obstacle.

**Buses and trucks**: This is where fuel cells are gaining traction fastest. Bus fleets operate on fixed routes (solving the infrastructure problem — you only need stations at the depot), carry heavy loads (where battery weight becomes a problem), and run all day (where battery charging downtime is costly). Over 3,000 fuel cell buses operate in China, and hundreds are in service across Europe and the U.S.

**Trains**: Several countries are deploying hydrogen-powered trains on routes where electrification (installing overhead wires) is too expensive. Alstom's Coradia iLint has been running in Germany since 2018.

**Maritime**: The shipping industry produces about 3% of global CO2 emissions and is exploring hydrogen and ammonia (which is easier to store and can be converted back to hydrogen) as alternatives to heavy fuel oil. For [automotive engineering](/automotive-engineering) and transport, hydrogen offers a path that batteries alone cannot provide.

**Aviation**: Airbus has announced plans for hydrogen-powered aircraft by 2035. Short-haul flights (under 1,000 miles) using hydrogen are considered technically feasible, though enormous engineering challenges remain around storage and safety.

### Stationary Power

Fuel cells can power buildings, data centers, and industrial facilities independently of the electrical grid. Bloom Energy has installed over 1 gigawatt of SOFC-based power at facilities for Apple, Google, Walmart, and others.

In Japan, over 400,000 residential fuel cell systems ("Ene-Farm" units) generate electricity and heat for individual homes, running on natural gas piped to the house. They achieve combined efficiencies above 90% by capturing waste heat for water heating.

For backup power, fuel cells offer advantages over diesel generators: no emissions, quieter operation, longer runtime than batteries, and lower maintenance. Telecom towers in remote locations increasingly use fuel cell backup systems.

### Portable and Military Applications

Portable fuel cells power equipment where batteries are too heavy or take too long to recharge. Military applications are significant — soldiers carrying batteries for electronics on multi-day missions face serious weight problems. Fuel cells with small hydrogen cartridges can provide more energy at less weight.

Drone operations also benefit. A fuel cell drone can fly 2-3 times longer than a battery-powered drone of the same weight, which matters for industrial inspection, [agriculture](/agriculture), and surveillance applications.

## The Economics: Getting Cheaper, Not There Yet

Cost has been the primary barrier to fuel cell adoption. But the numbers have improved dramatically:

- In 2006, an automotive PEM fuel cell cost about $124/kW
- By 2020, that dropped to about $45/kW
- The DOE target for cost parity with combustion engines is $30/kW
- Current projections suggest reaching this target by 2030 at scale production

Platinum catalyst cost is a major factor. Each automotive fuel cell uses about 30-60 grams of platinum (down from 100+ grams a decade ago). At roughly $30/gram, that's $900-$1,800 in platinum alone. Research into low-platinum and platinum-free catalysts is ongoing, with promising results from iron-nitrogen-carbon and cobalt-based alternatives.

The cost of hydrogen itself matters too. At $16/kg (typical U.S. retail price at the pump for green hydrogen), driving a fuel cell car costs about $0.21/mile — roughly comparable to gasoline but more than battery electric. As green hydrogen production scales up and drops toward $4-5/kg retail, the economics become much more attractive.

Government support is accelerating adoption. The U.S. Inflation Reduction Act (2022) provides tax credits of up to $3/kg for clean hydrogen production. The EU's hydrogen strategy targets 10 million tons of domestic green hydrogen production by 2030. Japan, South Korea, China, and Australia have all published national hydrogen strategies with billions in government investment.

## Challenges and Limitations

### Infrastructure

Building hydrogen infrastructure is expensive and complicated. A single hydrogen fueling station costs $1-3 million to build. Hydrogen is difficult to store (it's the smallest molecule and leaks through materials that contain other gases perfectly) and transport (it must be compressed to 700 bar or liquefied at -253°C for efficient transport).

The existing natural gas pipeline network could potentially be repurposed for hydrogen, but hydrogen embrittles certain steel alloys, meaning not all pipelines are compatible. New dedicated hydrogen pipelines are expensive to build.

### Storage

Storing hydrogen efficiently remains a [chemical engineering](/chemical-engineering) challenge. At standard conditions, hydrogen is a gas that takes up 3,000 times more space than gasoline containing the same energy. Compression (to 350 or 700 bar) and liquefaction (at -253°C) both require energy and specialized equipment.

Solid-state hydrogen storage — absorbing hydrogen into metal hydrides or other materials — could eventually solve the density problem but isn't yet practical for automotive applications.

### Durability

Fuel cells degrade over time. PEM fuel cells in vehicles currently achieve about 5,000-8,000 hours of operation before significant degradation. The DOE target is 8,000 hours for light-duty vehicles and 25,000 hours for heavy-duty (equivalent to about 150,000 and 500,000 miles respectively). Platinum catalyst degradation, membrane thinning, and electrode corrosion are the primary failure mechanisms.

Stationary fuel cells last longer — SOFC systems have demonstrated 40,000+ hours of operation — but material degradation at extreme temperatures remains a limiting factor.

## The Future of Fuel Cells

The next decade will likely determine fuel cell technology's role in the energy field. Several trends point toward significant growth:

- Green hydrogen costs are falling 40-50% per decade
- Electrolyzer manufacturing is scaling rapidly (from 8 GW/year in 2022 to projected 100+ GW/year by 2030)
- Heavy transport decarbonization creates natural demand for hydrogen fuel cells
- Industrial applications (steel, cement, chemicals) require hydrogen for decarbonization
- Seasonal energy storage (storing surplus [renewable energy](/renewable-energy) as hydrogen for winter use) has no practical [battery technology](/battery-technology) alternative at scale

The most likely scenario is not "batteries vs. fuel cells" but "batteries and fuel cells" — each technology serving the applications where it performs best. Passenger cars will largely go battery electric. Long-haul trucks, ships, aircraft, industrial heat, and grid-scale energy storage will increasingly turn to hydrogen and fuel cells.

## Key Takeaways

Fuel cell technology converts hydrogen and oxygen into electricity, water, and heat through an electrochemical reaction — no combustion, no carbon emissions at the point of use. Multiple fuel cell types serve different applications, from vehicles (PEM) to buildings (SOFC) to spacecraft (AFC).

The technology faces real challenges: hydrogen production is currently mostly fossil-fuel-based, infrastructure is limited, costs remain higher than alternatives for many applications, and round-trip efficiency is lower than direct electrification. But for heavy transport, industrial processes, and long-duration energy storage, fuel cells offer capabilities that batteries cannot match.

The transition to clean hydrogen — produced from renewable electricity — is the critical variable. As green hydrogen costs fall and production scales up, fuel cells will likely become a major component of the global clean energy system alongside batteries, solar, wind, and other low-carbon technologies.
