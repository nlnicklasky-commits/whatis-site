# What Is Furniture Making?

Furniture making is the craft of constructing furniture — chairs, tables, cabinets, beds, shelves, and desks — from wood, metal, and other materials. It's one of humanity's oldest crafts, predating written history. Egyptian tombs contained sophisticated furniture over 4,000 years old, and many of the joinery techniques used today were developed centuries ago. Despite mass manufacturing, handmade furniture remains valued for its quality, individuality, and the satisfaction of owning something made by human hands rather than machines.

## Wood — The Primary Material

Wood dominates furniture making for good reasons: it's strong relative to its weight, beautiful, workable with both hand and power tools, and available in thousands of species with different properties.

**Hardwoods** from deciduous trees — oak, walnut, cherry, maple, ash, mahogany — are the traditional choice for fine furniture. They're denser, more durable, and generally more attractive than softwoods. Each species has a distinctive grain pattern, color, and working character. Walnut is dark and richly figured. Cherry starts pale pink and darkens beautifully with age. Oak has bold, open grain that catches stain dramatically.

**Softwoods** from coniferous trees — pine, cedar, fir, spruce — are lighter, less expensive, and easier to work. Pine is the most common furniture softwood, used for everything from rustic tables to Scandinavian designs. It dents more easily than hardwood but works beautifully when properly finished.

**Engineered wood** — plywood, MDF, particleboard — serves important roles in modern furniture. Plywood is dimensionally stable (it doesn't expand and contract with humidity changes like solid wood) and available in large sheets. Veneered plywood combines the beauty of expensive hardwoods with the stability and economy of plywood.

## Joinery — The Heart of the Craft

Joinery is how pieces of wood are connected, and it's what separates furniture making from [carpentry](/carpentry). Good joints are strong, precise, and often beautiful in themselves.

**Mortise and tenon** — a rectangular protrusion (tenon) fits into a corresponding hole (mortise). This is the workhorse of furniture joinery, used for frame [construction](/construction), table legs, and chair assembly. Variations include through-tenons (visible through the other side) and wedged tenons (with a wedge that locks the joint tight).

**Dovetails** — interlocking trapezoidal pins and tails that resist being pulled apart. Traditionally used for drawer construction, dovetails are both functional and decorative. Hand-cut dovetails are a mark of craftsmanship; their slight irregularity is part of their appeal.

**Finger joints** (box joints) interlock like laced fingers. They're simpler than dovetails, provide excellent glue surface area, and look striking in contrasting woods.

**Biscuit and dowel joints** are modern alternatives that are faster to execute. A biscuit joiner cuts matching slots, and compressed wood biscuits expand when glued in place. Dowel joints use cylindrical wooden pegs in matching holes. Both are strong and practical for production work.

**Pocket screws** are the fastest joinery method — drilling an angled hole and driving a screw through it. Quick and sturdy for utilitarian furniture, though purists consider it cheating.

## Hand Tools vs. Power Tools

The perennial [debate](/debate): hand tools or machines?

**Hand tool [woodworking](/woodworking)** uses saws, planes, chisels, mallets, and hand drills. It's quieter, produces less dust, requires less space, and connects you physically to the material. A sharp hand plane produces a surface superior to any sandpaper. But hand work is slower and requires significant skill development.

**Power tool woodworking** uses table saws, routers, planers, jointers, drill presses, and sanders. Machines cut faster, produce more consistent results, and enable operations that would be extremely difficult by hand. But they're expensive, loud, dusty, and dangerous — table saws alone cause over 30,000 injuries per year in the U.S.

Most furniture makers use both. Machines for dimensioning lumber (cutting to size, flattening, thicknessing) and hand tools for joinery, shaping, and finishing. The combination plays to each approach's strengths.

## The Making Process

Building a piece of furniture follows a general workflow.

**Design and planning** determine dimensions, materials, joinery methods, and finish. Experienced makers often build from sketches; beginners benefit from detailed plans with cut lists and assembly sequences.

**Milling** converts rough lumber into flat, square, consistently thick boards. This involves jointing one face flat, planing to thickness, ripping to width, and crosscutting to length. Milling is the foundation — everything that follows depends on starting with flat, square stock.

**Joinery** is cut with precision. Mortises, tenons, dovetails, or other joints are marked carefully, cut slightly oversized, and fitted by hand to achieve a snug connection. This is the most skill-intensive phase.

**Assembly** brings pieces together, usually with glue and sometimes with mechanical fasteners. Dry-fitting (assembling without glue to check fit) is essential. Once glue goes on, you have minutes before it sets — so the sequence must be planned and all clamps ready.

**Finishing** protects the wood and enhances its appearance. Options include oil finishes (penetrating oils that soak into the wood), film finishes (lacquer, varnish, or polyurethane that form a protective layer), wax, and paint. Each has different looks, durability, and application characteristics.

## Getting Started

Furniture making is accessible. A first project — a simple shelf, stool, or small table — requires minimal tools and teaches fundamental skills. Mistakes are inevitable and educational. Wood is forgiving in the sense that most errors can be worked around (and unforgiving in the sense that once you cut too short, it stays too short).

Resources abound: YouTube channels (Paul Sellers, Wood Whisperer, Jonathan Katz-Moses), community workshops (makerspaces), community college courses, and books. Starting simple and building skills progressively is better than attempting ambitious projects too early.

The reward is tangible. When you eat dinner at a table you built, sit in a chair you constructed, or store books on shelves you made — there's a quiet satisfaction that store-bought furniture can't provide. You made this. It works. It might even be beautiful. That's enough.
