# What Is Mythology?

Mythology is a body of myths — traditional stories that a culture uses to explain the world, its origins, natural phenomena, and the human condition. Every known civilization has produced mythology. The Greeks had Zeus and Athena. The Norse had Odin and Thor. The Egyptians had Ra and Osiris. The Hindu tradition has Vishnu, Shiva, and thousands of others. Indigenous peoples across every continent developed mythological systems of tremendous complexity and beauty.

These aren't just old stories. Myths are how pre-scientific cultures made sense of a confusing, dangerous, and often terrifying world. Why does the sun move across the sky? Why do people die? Why do seasons change? Where did we come from? What happens after death? Myths answered these questions — and their answers, while not scientifically accurate, reveal enormous psychological and philosophical sophistication.

## What Myths Do

Myths serve several overlapping functions:

**Explaining origins** — creation myths (cosmogonies) are universal. Nearly every culture has a story about how the world began. The Norse imagined a void between fire and ice. The Babylonian *Enuma Elish* describes the world formed from the body of a slain goddess. Genesis describes creation through divine speech. These stories differ wildly in detail but share a common project: giving the universe a narrative beginning.

**Explaining nature** — why does it rain? Thunder? Why do volcanoes erupt? Before science provided mechanistic explanations, myths offered narrative ones. The Greek god Helios drove the sun across the sky in a chariot. The Japanese storm god Susanoo caused tempests. Pele, the Hawaiian goddess, lived in Kilauea's crater. These explanations weren't naive — they embedded practical knowledge (Pele's stories, for instance, encoded accurate information about volcanic behavior).

**Establishing moral frameworks** — myths teach right behavior through example and counter-example. The Greek myth of Icarus (who flew too close to the sun on wax wings) warns against hubris. The Norse myth of Loki teaches that cleverness without loyalty destroys everything it touches. Hindu mythology explores dharma (duty and moral order) through complex, morally ambiguous narratives.

**Connecting humans to the sacred** — myths create a bridge between the everyday world and something larger. Ritual performance of myths — [acting](/acting) them out in ceremonies, festivals, and religious practices — allowed people to participate in sacred time, stepping out of ordinary existence and into the mythological area.

## Major Mythological Traditions

**[Greek mythology](/greek-mythology)** — the most familiar in the Western world, thanks to its influence on art, [literature](/literature), [philosophy](/philosophy), and language. The Olympian gods (Zeus, Hera, Athena, Apollo, Aphrodite, and others) are deeply human — jealous, petty, lustful, vengeful, and occasionally generous. Greek myths explore fate, pride, love, revenge, and the tension between divine power and human agency. Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* (c. 8th century BCE) are the foundational texts.

**[Norse mythology](/norse-mythology)** — from the Scandinavian and Germanic peoples. A darker, more fatalistic tradition. The gods know they will die at Ragnarok (the end of the world) and fight anyway. Odin sacrifices an eye for wisdom. Thor battles the world serpent. Loki, the trickster, engineers catastrophe. The *Prose Edda* and *Poetic Edda* (13th century, [drawing](/drawing) on older oral traditions) are the primary sources.

**[Egyptian mythology](/egyptian-mythology)** — one of the oldest documented traditions, spanning over 3,000 years. Central myths include Ra's daily journey through the sky and underworld, Osiris's death and resurrection, and Isis's devotion and magical power. Egyptian myth was inseparable from [religion](/religion) — temples, rituals, and the entire social order were organized around mythological frameworks.

**Hindu mythology** — one of the richest and most complex mythological systems, and one of the few that remains actively practiced. The *Mahabharata* (over 100,000 verses, the world's longest epic) and the *Ramayana* explore duty, honor, love, and cosmic order through the stories of gods, heroes, and demons. Hindu mythology embraces contradiction and paradox in ways that Western traditions often don't.

**Mesoamerican mythology** — the Aztec, Maya, and other Mesoamerican civilizations produced sophisticated mythological systems. The Popol Vuh (K'iche' Maya creation narrative) describes multiple attempts by the gods to create humanity. Quetzalcoatl (the feathered serpent) appears across Mesoamerican cultures. These traditions were largely suppressed during European colonization but survive in texts and living cultural practices.

**[Chinese mythology](/chinese-mythology)** — an ancient tradition blending Daoist, Buddhist, and folk elements. The Jade Emperor rules heaven. The Monkey King (Sun Wukong), immortalized in *Journey to the West*, is one of the most beloved mythological figures in East Asia. Chinese mythology often emphasizes [harmony](/harmony) between heaven, earth, and humanity.

## Mythology and Modern Culture

Myths never went away — they changed form. Joseph Campbell's *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949) argued that myths worldwide share a common narrative structure: the hero's journey (departure, initiation, return). George Lucas explicitly used Campbell's framework to structure *Star Wars*. The Marvel Cinematic Universe is essentially a modern mythology featuring characters drawn directly from Norse and Greek traditions.

Tolkien's *Lord of the Rings* deliberately created a mythology for England. Neil Gaiman's *American Gods* explores what happens to old-world myths in modern America. Rick Riordan's Percy Jackson series introduced Greek mythology to millions of young readers.

Psychologically, Carl Jung argued that mythological motifs (archetypes) — the hero, the shadow, the trickster, the wise old man, the great mother — exist in every human psyche. Whether or not Jung's specific theory is correct, the cross-cultural recurrence of similar mythological patterns is striking. Flood myths appear on every inhabited continent. Trickster figures appear in nearly every tradition. Creation from chaos is almost universal.

## Why Myths Still Matter

We live in a scientific age. We know why the sun crosses the sky, why seasons change, and (mostly) how the universe began. So why study mythology?

Because myths aren't really about explaining lightning. They're about explaining us — our fears, our desires, our relationships with each other and with death. Achilles' rage in the *Iliad* isn't about Bronze Age warfare. It's about what happens when pride overrides love. Persephone's descent to the underworld isn't just a seasonal allegory. It's about loss, growth, and the impossibility of going home unchanged.

Science tells you how the world works. Mythology tells you what it feels like to be alive in it. Both are necessary. Neither is sufficient alone.
