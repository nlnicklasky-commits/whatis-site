# What Is Organic Chemistry?

Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-based compounds — their structures, properties, reactions, and how to build them from scratch. It's the branch of [chemistry](/chemistry) that explains how drugs work, why gasoline burns, how plastics are made, and what makes DNA the blueprint of life.

## Why Carbon Gets Its Own Chemistry

Out of 118 elements on the periodic table, carbon gets an entire branch of chemistry to itself. That might seem unfair. But there's a very good reason: carbon is absurdly versatile.

Carbon has four valence electrons, meaning it forms four covalent bonds. It bonds readily to itself, to hydrogen, to oxygen, nitrogen, sulfur, phosphorus, and halogens. It forms single bonds, double bonds, and triple bonds. It creates chains, branches, rings, cages, sheets, and tubes. No other element comes close to this structural diversity.

The result? Over 100 million known organic compounds, dwarfing the roughly 500,000 known inorganic compounds. And the number keeps growing — chemists synthesize thousands of new organic molecules every week.

This structural diversity is why life chose carbon as its building block. Every protein in your body, every strand of DNA, every cell membrane, every neurotransmitter firing in your brain right now — all organic molecules. Carbon's bonding flexibility allows the complex, precisely shaped molecules that biological processes require.

### A Quick Historical Detour

For most of history, chemists believed organic compounds could only be produced by living organisms through a mysterious "vital force." This idea — vitalism — seemed reasonable. You could extract sugar from sugarcane and alcohol from fermented grains, but you couldn't create them from rocks.

Then in 1828, Friedrich Wohler heated ammonium cyanate (an inorganic salt) and produced urea (an organic compound found in urine). No living organism involved. Vitalism started crumbling. By the mid-1800s, chemists were synthesizing organic compounds routinely, and the field exploded.

August Kekule's proposal of benzene's ring structure in 1865 (he reportedly dreamed of a snake biting its own tail) and the development of structural formulas transformed organic chemistry from a descriptive catalog into a predictive science. Once chemists could draw molecular structures, they could start predicting how molecules would react — and designing new ones on purpose.

## The Language of Organic Chemistry: Functional Groups

Organic molecules can be enormous — proteins contain thousands of atoms — but their behavior is determined by relatively small clusters of atoms called functional groups. Learn the functional groups, and you can predict how almost any organic molecule will behave. They're the vocabulary of the field.

**Hydroxyl group (-OH)**: Found in alcohols. Ethanol (drinking alcohol) is CH3CH2OH. The OH group makes molecules more water-soluble and reactive. Methanol (CH3OH) is toxic; ethanol gives you a hangover; isopropanol is rubbing alcohol. Same functional group, very different consequences depending on what it's attached to.

**Carbonyl group (C=O)**: The workhorse of organic reactions. Found in aldehydes (like formaldehyde), ketones (like acetone — nail polish remover), carboxylic acids (like acetic acid in vinegar), and esters (responsible for many fruit flavors and fragrances).

**Amine group (-NH2)**: Found in amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Amines are also present in many drugs, neurotransmitters (dopamine, serotonin), and DNA bases. They're typically basic (they accept protons), which affects their solubility and reactivity.

**Carboxyl group (-COOH)**: Combines a carbonyl and a hydroxyl. Found in amino acids, fatty acids, aspirin, and citric acid. Carboxylic acids are weak acids — they partially donate a proton in water, giving vinegar its sour taste and citrus its zing.

**Ester group (-COO-)**: Formed when a carboxylic acid reacts with an alcohol. Esters give bananas, strawberries, and oranges their characteristic smells. Polyesters (like PET plastic) are long chains linked by ester bonds. Fats and oils are triglycerides — three fatty acid chains linked to glycerol by ester bonds.

**Amide group (-CONH-)**: The link that connects amino acids into proteins. The peptide bond is an amide bond. Nylon, Kevlar, and other polyamides use the same linkage in synthetic materials.

There are dozens more — ethers, thiols, phosphates, sulfoxides — but these core groups account for the majority of organic chemistry you'll encounter.

## How Organic Reactions Work: Mechanisms

This is where organic chemistry gets genuinely interesting. Reactions don't just happen randomly — they follow specific, predictable pathways called mechanisms. Understanding mechanisms means you can predict products, explain why reactions fail, and design new synthetic routes.

### Electrons Are the Currency

Every organic reaction involves electrons moving from one place to another. Bonds form when electrons are shared between atoms. Bonds break when electrons leave. The fundamental question of any organic mechanism is: where do the electrons go?

Nucleophiles are electron-rich species — they have electrons to give. Electrophiles are electron-poor species — they want electrons. Most organic reactions involve a nucleophile attacking an electrophile. That's it. That's the core pattern underlying thousands of different reactions.

### Substitution Reactions

In a substitution reaction, one group replaces another on a carbon atom. There are two main mechanisms.

**SN2** (substitution, nucleophilic, bimolecular): The nucleophile attacks the carbon from the back side while the leaving group departs from the front. It happens in one step — a synchronized arrival and departure. Think of it like pushing one car out of a parking spot by driving another one in. SN2 reactions invert the stereochemistry (the 3D arrangement) of the carbon, which matters enormously in [pharmaceutical chemistry](/pharmaceutical-chemistry) where mirror-image molecules can have drastically different biological effects.

**SN1** (substitution, nucleophilic, unimolecular): The leaving group departs first, creating a positively charged carbon (carbocation). Then the nucleophile attacks. Two steps, with the rate depending only on the first step. SN1 reactions don't invert stereochemistry — the nucleophile can attack from either side, giving a mixture.

Which mechanism dominates depends on the substrate, the nucleophile, the solvent, and the leaving group. Predicting this is bread-and-butter organic chemistry.

### Elimination Reactions

Instead of substituting one group for another, elimination reactions remove two groups and form a double bond. When you dehydrate ethanol (remove water) to make ethylene, that's an elimination reaction. Elimination and substitution often compete — controlling which pathway dominates is a constant challenge in synthesis.

### Addition Reactions

The reverse of elimination. A double or triple bond opens up and new atoms attach. This is how polyethylene is made: thousands of ethylene molecules (CH2=CH2) undergo addition reactions, their double bonds opening and linking into a long chain. It's also how your body adds hydrogen to unsaturated fats — hydrogenation.

### Radical Reactions

Most organic reactions involve pairs of electrons, but radical reactions involve single unpaired electrons. Radicals are extremely reactive and are responsible for combustion (burning), polymer chain reactions, atmospheric ozone depletion, and biological aging. Free radical damage to DNA is one mechanism of cancer development, which is partly why antioxidants — radical scavengers — are valued in [nutrition](/nutrition).

## Stereochemistry: Shape Matters

One of the most profound insights of organic chemistry is that molecular shape matters just as much as molecular formula. Two molecules can have identical atoms connected in identical order and still behave completely differently because of their three-dimensional arrangement.

**Chirality** is the most important concept here. A carbon atom bonded to four different groups is chiral — it exists in two mirror-image forms (enantiomers) that can't be superimposed, like left and right hands. Your left hand and right hand have identical "atoms" in identical connections, but they're not interchangeable. Try putting a left glove on your right hand.

This matters enormously in [biology](/biology). Enzymes, receptors, and DNA are all chiral. They interact differently with left-handed and right-handed versions of a drug molecule. The painkiller ibuprofen exists as two enantiomers — the S-enantiomer is the active form, while the R-enantiomer is essentially inert. The notorious drug thalidomide caused birth defects because one enantiomer was therapeutic while the other was teratogenic.

Modern pharmaceutical chemistry is obsessed with chirality. About 50% of all drugs are chiral, and regulations increasingly require companies to develop single-enantiomer versions. Asymmetric synthesis — selectively making one enantiomer over the other — won the 2001 Nobel Prize in Chemistry (Knowles, Noyori, and Sharpless) and the 2021 Nobel Prize (List and MacMillan for organocatalysis).

## Organic Synthesis: Building Molecules on Purpose

Synthesis is the art and science of constructing target molecules from simpler starting materials. It's where organic chemistry becomes genuinely creative.

### Total Synthesis: The Ultimate Challenge

Total synthesis means building a complex natural product entirely from simple, commercially available chemicals. It's the organic chemistry equivalent of building a cathedral from raw stone.

Robert Woodward synthesized cholesterol in 1951, quinine in 1944, and vitamin B12 in 1972 — feats that won him the 1965 Nobel Prize and established synthesis as a supreme intellectual challenge. Elias James Corey formalized the [logic](/logic) of synthesis planning with retrosynthetic analysis — working backward from the target molecule, identifying strategic bond disconnections that simplify the structure. He won the 1990 Nobel Prize for this work.

Modern total synthesis tackles molecules of staggering complexity. Palytoxin, a marine toxin with 64 stereocenters and 7 double bonds of defined [geometry](/geometry), was synthesized by Yoshito Kishi's group. The probability of getting the right stereoisomer by chance is about 1 in 10^19. That they could make specifically the correct one is a proof to the precision of modern synthetic methods.

### Pharmaceutical Synthesis

Drug development depends on organic synthesis at every stage. Medicinal chemists synthesize thousands of analogs (variations) of lead compounds, testing each for potency, selectivity, toxicity, and pharmacokinetics. [Medicinal chemistry](/medicinal-chemistry) is applied organic chemistry with an urgent practical purpose.

The synthesis of penicillin during [World War II](/world-war-ii) — scaling from milligram laboratory quantities to tons of industrial production — changed medicine forever. Today, synthesizing a single drug candidate for clinical trials can cost $50-100 million before a single patient takes it.

### Industrial Organic Chemistry

The global chemical industry produces hundreds of millions of tons of organic chemicals annually. Ethylene (polyethylene plastic, antifreeze), propylene (polypropylene, acrylic), benzene (nylon, polystyrene), and methanol (formaldehyde, fuels) are produced on scales exceeding 100 million tons per year each.

Petroleum refining is fundamentally organic chemistry — cracking large hydrocarbons into smaller ones, reforming straight chains into branched ones, and removing sulfur. The transition away from fossil fuels will require new organic chemistry for bio-based plastics, synthetic fuels, and advanced battery materials.

## [Spectroscopy](/spectroscopy): Identifying What You've Made

You can't see molecules directly. So how do organic chemists know what they've made? Through spectroscopy — using different types of electromagnetic radiation to probe molecular structure.

**Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR)**: The single most important tool. NMR exploits the magnetic properties of atomic nuclei (especially hydrogen and carbon-13) to reveal molecular structure. Different hydrogen atoms in a molecule produce signals at different frequencies, telling you what environments those hydrogens are in. A trained chemist can determine a molecule's complete structure from its NMR spectra alone. The technique is so valuable that about 60% of chemistry Nobel Prizes since 1990 have involved NMR in some way.

**Mass Spectrometry (MS)**: Determines molecular weight and formula by ionizing the molecule and measuring the mass-to-charge ratio of the fragments. Modern mass spectrometers can determine molecular formulas to four decimal places, distinguishing between molecules that differ by a single hydrogen atom.

**Infrared Spectroscopy (IR)**: Functional groups absorb infrared light at characteristic frequencies. A sharp absorption at 1700 cm^-1 signals a carbonyl group. A broad absorption around 3300 cm^-1 indicates an O-H or N-H bond. IR is quick and useful for confirming the presence or absence of specific functional groups.

**[X-ray Crystallography](/x-ray-crystallography))**: When you can grow a crystal of your compound, X-ray diffraction gives you the exact three-dimensional arrangement of every atom. This technique determined the structures of DNA (Rosalind Franklin's famous Photo 51), penicillin (Dorothy Hodgkin), and thousands of proteins. It's definitive but requires crystals, which aren't always easy to obtain.

## Organic Chemistry Meets Biology

The boundary between organic chemistry and [biochemistry](/biochemistry) is blurry and getting blurrier.

**Proteins** are polymers of amino acids linked by peptide (amide) bonds. Their function depends entirely on their three-dimensional shape, which is determined by the organic chemistry of their side chains — hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic interactions, disulfide bridges, salt bridges.

**DNA and RNA** are polymers of nucleotides linked by phosphodiester bonds. The genetic code is stored in the sequence of four organic bases: adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine (or uracil in RNA). The double helix is held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary bases — A pairs with T, G pairs with C. Watson and Crick figured this out in 1953, one of the most consequential applications of organic chemistry ever.

**Lipids** — fats, oils, cholesterol, cell membranes — are organic molecules whose properties are determined by hydrocarbon chain length, saturation (number of double bonds), and functional group headgroups. The cell membrane is a lipid bilayer whose fluidity is controlled by its organic chemistry: more unsaturated fats = more fluid membrane.

**Carbohydrates** — sugars, starch, cellulose — are polyhydroxylated aldehydes and ketones. Glucose, the primary energy currency of cells, is a six-carbon aldehyde with five hydroxyl groups. Its [stereochemistry](/chemistry) matters: D-glucose is metabolized efficiently; L-glucose passes through your body undigested.

## Modern Frontiers

Organic chemistry in 2026 looks very different from even a decade ago.

**Green chemistry** aims to reduce waste and eliminate hazardous substances from chemical synthesis. Catalytic methods that use tiny amounts of metal catalysts instead of stoichiometric reagents, reactions performed in water instead of toxic solvents, and bio-based feedstocks are all active areas. The twelve principles of green chemistry, articulated by Paul Anastas and John Warner in 1998, have become a framework for sustainable synthesis.

**Flow chemistry** replaces traditional batch reactions (mix everything in a flask and heat) with continuous flow through narrow tubes. This gives better temperature control, faster mixing, safer handling of dangerous reagents, and easier scale-up from lab to factory. Pharmaceutical companies are increasingly adopting flow processes.

**Machine learning for synthesis** uses [artificial intelligence](/artificial-intelligence) to predict reaction outcomes and plan synthetic routes. Programs like IBM's RXN for Chemistry and Synthia (from Merck) can suggest multi-step synthesis plans for target molecules in seconds — a task that once required a PhD chemist hours of thought. They're not perfect, but they're improving rapidly.

**Bioorthogonal chemistry** develops reactions that work inside living cells without interfering with biological processes. Carolyn Bertozzi won the 2022 Nobel Prize for pioneering click chemistry and bioorthogonal reactions, enabling researchers to label and track specific molecules inside living organisms. This has applications in drug delivery, diagnostics, and understanding [cell biology](/cell-biology).

**Organic [electronics](/electronics)** uses conjugated organic molecules and polymers as semiconductors. Organic LEDs (OLEDs) are already in millions of smartphones and TVs. Organic solar cells and organic transistors are advancing rapidly, potentially enabling flexible, printable, and cheap electronic devices.

## Why Organic Chemistry Matters to You

Even if you never set foot in a chemistry lab, organic chemistry affects every moment of your life.

The medicine you take, the food you eat, the clothes you wear (most textiles are either natural organic polymers like cotton or synthetic ones like polyester), the fuel in your car, the plastic in your phone case, the dye in your shirt, the flavor in your coffee, the fragrance of your soap — all organic chemistry.

Your body runs on organic chemistry. Every breath you take oxidizes organic molecules (glucose) to produce energy. Every thought you think involves organic neurotransmitters binding to organic receptors. The DNA replicating in your cells right now is organic chemistry in action.

Understanding organic chemistry — even at a basic level — helps you make sense of nutrition claims, drug advertisements, environmental debates about plastics and pesticides, and the material choices that shape your daily life. It's not just an academic subject. It's the chemistry of everything that lives, burns, grows, heals, or decays.

## Key Takeaways

Organic chemistry is the science of carbon-based compounds, covering their structure, reactions, synthesis, and properties. Carbon's unique ability to form four bonds in diverse arrangements creates a universe of over 100 million known molecules. Functional groups determine reactivity, stereochemistry determines biological activity, and spectroscopy reveals structure. The field supports pharmaceuticals, [materials science](/materials-science), energy, and biology — making it one of the most practically important sciences. Modern frontiers include green chemistry, AI-assisted synthesis, bioorthogonal chemistry, and organic electronics.
