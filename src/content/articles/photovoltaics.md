# What Is Photovoltaics?

Photovoltaics (PV) is the technology that converts sunlight directly into electricity using semiconductor materials. When photons from the sun hit a solar cell, they knock electrons loose from atoms, creating an electrical current. No moving parts, no combustion, no noise. Just light in, electricity out.

That simple description belies one of the most consequential technological stories of the 21st century.

## The Physics Behind the Magic

The photovoltaic effect was discovered by French physicist Edmond Becquerel in 1839 --- he was 19 years old at the time, which is frankly absurd. He observed that certain materials produced small amounts of electric current when exposed to light. But it took over a century before anyone could turn that observation into something useful.

### How a Solar Cell Actually Works

A typical solar cell is a sandwich of semiconductor material --- usually silicon, the same element that powers computer chips. Here's what happens, step by step.

**Step 1: Doping the silicon.** Pure silicon is a lousy conductor. To make it useful, manufacturers add tiny amounts of other elements --- a process called doping. Adding phosphorus creates n-type silicon (extra electrons, negative carriers). Adding boron creates p-type silicon (missing electrons, creating "holes" that act as positive carriers).

**Step 2: Creating the junction.** When you put n-type and p-type silicon together, something remarkable happens at the boundary. Electrons from the n-type side migrate to the p-type side, and holes migrate the other way. This creates an electric field at the junction --- a built-in voltage that acts like a one-way gate for electron flow.

**Step 3: Photon absorption.** When a photon of sufficient energy hits the silicon, it knocks an electron free from its atomic bond, creating an electron-hole pair. The photon's energy must exceed silicon's band gap (1.12 electron volts, corresponding to near-infrared light) for this to happen. Photons with less energy pass right through. Photons with more energy create the electron-hole pair but waste the excess energy as heat.

**Step 4: Charge separation.** The electric field at the p-n junction sweeps the freed electron toward the n-type side and the hole toward the p-type side. This separation is what creates voltage.

**Step 5: Current collection.** Metal contacts on the front and back of the cell collect the separated charges. Connect a wire between them, and electrons flow through the external circuit --- that's your electricity. The electron eventually returns to the cell and recombines with a hole, completing the circuit.

This entire process happens silently, with no mechanical motion, in a fraction of a microsecond. And it keeps happening as long as photons keep arriving.

### The Efficiency Challenge

Here's the fundamental frustration of photovoltaics: the sun delivers about 1,000 watts per square meter at Earth's surface, but no single-junction solar cell can convert more than about 33.7% of that into electricity. This limit, calculated by William Shockley and Hans-Joachim Queisser in 1961, is one of the most important numbers in solar energy.

Why the limit? Several reasons. Photons below the band gap energy can't create electron-hole pairs (they're wasted). Photons above the band gap create electron-hole pairs but dump the excess energy as heat (also wasted). Some light reflects off the surface. Some electron-hole pairs recombine before being collected. Together, these losses set a hard ceiling for single-junction cells.

The Shockley-Queisser limit applies only to single-junction cells. Multi-junction cells stack different semiconductor materials, each tuned to absorb different wavelengths, and can theoretically exceed 60% efficiency. Lab records for multi-junction cells already surpass 47%.

## Types of Solar Cells

Not all solar cells are created equal. The three main categories represent different tradeoffs between cost, efficiency, and practicality.

### Monocrystalline Silicon

These are the black or dark blue panels you see on most rooftops. Each cell is cut from a single crystal of silicon, which gives electrons the most orderly path through the material. Commercial efficiency: 20-24%. They're the most efficient mainstream technology but also the most expensive to manufacture because growing large single crystals requires high temperatures and careful processing.

The Czochralski process, developed in 1916, remains the primary method: a seed crystal is slowly pulled from a vat of molten silicon, creating a cylindrical ingot that's sliced into wafers about 150-180 micrometers thick.

### Polycrystalline Silicon

These panels have a distinctive blue, speckled appearance because each cell contains many small silicon crystals rather than one large one. The boundaries between crystals impede electron flow, reducing efficiency to 15-19%. But they're cheaper to produce because you skip the expensive single-crystal growing step --- you just melt silicon and let it solidify in a mold.

For years, polycrystalline panels dominated the market on price. But monocrystalline manufacturing costs have dropped so dramatically that mono panels now dominate globally, offering better efficiency per square meter.

### Thin-Film Technologies

Thin-film cells deposit extremely thin layers of semiconductor material (a few micrometers) onto glass, plastic, or metal substrates. Three main types exist:

**Cadmium telluride (CdTe)** is the most commercially successful thin-film technology. First Solar, the leading manufacturer, produces panels at some of the lowest costs in the industry. Efficiency: 18-19% commercially. The downside: cadmium is toxic, raising manufacturing and disposal concerns.

**Copper indium gallium selenide (CIGS)** offers higher lab efficiencies (over 23%) but has proven difficult to manufacture consistently at scale.

**Amorphous silicon (a-Si)** uses non-crystalline silicon. It's the cheapest to produce and works well in low-light conditions, but efficiency is only 6-9%. You've seen it in calculators and small electronics.

### Emerging Technologies

The most exciting developments are happening outside traditional silicon.

**Perovskite solar cells** use a class of crystal structures that can be deposited from solution at low temperatures --- essentially printing solar cells like newspaper. Lab efficiency has rocketed from 3.8% in 2009 to over 26% in 2024, the fastest improvement in photovoltaic history. The challenge: perovskites degrade quickly when exposed to moisture, heat, and UV light. Solving the stability problem could make perovskites the most disruptive solar technology in decades.

**Tandem cells** stack perovskite on top of silicon, capturing wavelengths that each material alone would waste. Perovskite-silicon tandems have reached 33.9% efficiency in the lab --- well above the Shockley-Queisser limit for single-junction cells. This technology could be commercially available within a few years.

**Organic photovoltaics** use carbon-based polymers and small molecules. They're flexible, lightweight, and potentially very cheap. Efficiency is lower (around 19% in the lab), but they could be integrated into windows, building facades, and clothing.

**Quantum dot cells** use nanoscale semiconductor particles whose properties can be tuned by changing their size. They're still largely in the research phase but offer the theoretical possibility of exceeding single-junction efficiency limits through multiple exciton generation.

## From Cell to System: How Solar Installations Work

A single solar cell produces about 0.5 volts --- not enough to power much of anything. Real-world solar power requires assembling cells into systems.

### Modules and Arrays

Individual cells are wired together into modules (what most people call "solar panels"). A typical residential module contains 60-72 cells and produces 300-400 watts. Modules are connected into arrays, and the array size determines total power output.

### Inverters

Solar cells produce direct current (DC), but your home runs on alternating current (AC). Inverters convert DC to AC. String inverters connect multiple panels in series and convert at a central location. Microinverters attach to individual panels, optimizing each one independently --- better for partially shaded installations but more expensive.

### Balance of System

The non-panel components --- wiring, mounting hardware, monitoring systems, and safety disconnects --- are collectively called "balance of system." These account for roughly half the cost of a residential solar installation, which is why panel efficiency matters: higher-efficiency panels mean fewer panels needed, which means less mounting hardware, less wiring, and less roof space.

### Energy Storage

Solar produces electricity when the sun shines, which doesn't always match when you need it. Battery storage systems (typically lithium-ion) store excess daytime production for evening use. The cost of lithium-ion batteries dropped about 97% between 1991 and 2023, making home storage increasingly practical.

Grid-connected systems without batteries can net meter --- sending excess electricity to the grid during the day and drawing from it at night. This effectively uses the grid as a battery, though net metering policies vary by location and are politically contentious.

## The Economics: Why Solar Exploded

The story of solar economics is one of the most dramatic cost declines in technology history.

In 1976, solar panels cost about $106 per watt. By 2024, the global average was under $0.20 per watt --- a decline of over 99.8%. This follows "Swanson's Law," which observes that the price of solar panels drops roughly 20% for every doubling of cumulative shipped volume.

What drove this? Manufacturing scale, primarily. China invested heavily in solar manufacturing starting in the mid-2000s, and global production capacity grew from about 2 GW per year in 2005 to over 450 GW per year in 2023. This massive scaling reduced costs through learning-by-doing, process optimization, and supply chain maturation.

The result: solar is now the cheapest source of new electricity generation in most of the world. The International Energy Agency's 2023 World Energy Outlook found that solar PV is the cheapest source of new electricity in countries representing 95% of global power generation. In many locations, building new solar is cheaper than continuing to operate existing coal plants.

Global installed solar capacity reached approximately 1,600 GW by the end of 2023, generating about 6% of global electricity. The IEA projects solar will become the single largest source of electricity globally by 2030.

## Manufacturing: How Solar Panels Are Made

Understanding how panels are manufactured helps explain both their declining costs and their environmental footprint.

### Silicon Production

It starts with quartz sand (silicon dioxide), which is smelted in an arc furnace at about 1,800 degrees Celsius to produce metallurgical-grade silicon (98-99% pure). This is then purified to semiconductor-grade silicon (99.9999% pure) through the Siemens process, which involves converting silicon to trichlorosilane gas and redepositing it.

This purification step is energy-intensive --- producing a kilogram of polysilicon requires roughly 100-120 kWh of electricity. China's dominance in solar manufacturing is partly explained by its access to cheap coal-fired electricity in regions like Xinjiang and Inner Mongolia, which raises uncomfortable questions about the carbon footprint of solar panel production.

### Cell Fabrication

Silicon wafers are processed into cells through a series of steps: texturing the surface to reduce reflection, creating the p-n junction through diffusion or ion implantation, applying anti-reflective coatings, and screen-printing metal contacts. Modern cell designs like PERC (Passivated Emitter and Rear Cell) and TOPCon (Tunnel Oxide Passivated Contact) add additional layers to reduce electron recombination and boost efficiency.

### Module Assembly

Cells are soldered together, laminated between sheets of glass and polymer (typically EVA or POE), framed in aluminum, and fitted with a junction box and wiring. Quality control includes flash testing (measuring electrical output), electroluminescence imaging (detecting defects invisible to the naked eye), and mechanical stress testing.

## Environmental Considerations

Solar energy produces no emissions during operation, but the full lifecycle picture is more subtle.

### Energy Payback Time

A solar panel takes energy to manufacture. The energy payback time --- how long it must operate to generate more energy than went into making it --- is typically 1-3 years depending on location and technology. Given a 25-30 year lifespan, panels produce 8-25 times the energy used in their manufacture.

### Carbon Footprint

Lifecycle carbon emissions from solar PV are roughly 20-50 grams of CO2 per kilowatt-hour, compared to about 820 g/kWh for coal and 490 g/kWh for natural gas. Solar's carbon footprint is dominated by manufacturing, especially silicon purification.

### Material Concerns

Solar panels use silver for contacts (about 10-15 grams per panel), which contributes to cost and raises questions about silver supply constraints at terawatt scale. Research is actively pursuing copper and aluminum alternatives. Cadmium in CdTe panels and lead in perovskites raise toxicity concerns, though amounts are small and containment during operation is effective.

### Land Use

Utility-scale solar farms require 5-10 acres per megawatt. A study by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory found that powering the entire US electricity system with solar would require about 22,000 square miles --- roughly 0.6% of US land area. That's significant but manageable, especially since much could be placed on rooftops, parking lots, and degraded land.

Agrivoltaics --- farming under and around solar panels --- is gaining traction. Certain crops actually benefit from partial shade, and the panels benefit from the cooling effect of plant transpiration.

## Grid Integration Challenges

Solar's intermittency --- it only works when the sun shines --- creates real challenges for electrical grids.

**Duck curve**: In regions with heavy solar penetration, net demand (total demand minus solar generation) drops sharply during the day and ramps steeply in the evening as solar output falls and people come home. This "duck curve," named for its shape, strains conventional power plants that must ramp up quickly.

**Curtailment**: On sunny spring days with low demand, solar can produce more electricity than the grid needs. Without sufficient storage, this excess must be curtailed (wasted). California curtailed about 2.4 million MWh of solar in 2023.

**Solutions** include battery storage, demand response (shifting electricity use to match solar production), transmission upgrades (connecting sunny regions to distant demand centers), and overbuilding solar capacity paired with electrolysis to produce green hydrogen from excess electricity.

## Where Solar Goes From Here

Solar photovoltaics has gone from a curiosity powering space satellites in the 1960s to the world's fastest-growing source of electricity. The technology isn't waiting around --- tandem cells, perovskites, building-integrated PV, and floating solar installations are all expanding what's possible.

The numbers tell the story. In 2004, the world installed about 1 GW of solar per year. In 2023, it installed over 400 GW. By 2030, annual installations are expected to exceed 1,000 GW. At that pace, solar will generate a quarter of the world's electricity before most children born today finish school.

It's not a perfect technology. It needs storage, it needs grid upgrades, and manufacturing has environmental costs. But sunlight is free, abundant, and available everywhere on Earth. Converting it to electricity with no moving parts and steadily declining costs is, by any reasonable measure, one of humanity's better ideas.
