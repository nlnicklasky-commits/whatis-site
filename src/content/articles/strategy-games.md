# What Is Strategy Games?

A strategy game is any game where the player's decisions, planning, and resource management are the primary determinants of success, rather than reflexes, physical skill, or luck. Strategy games put you in the role of a commander, ruler, general, or manager — someone who sees the big picture and makes choices that ripple forward in time.

## What Makes a Game "Strategic"

Not every game with choices is a strategy game. The distinction lies in *what kind* of choices matter most.

In an action game, success depends primarily on timing, reflexes, and hand-eye coordination. In a puzzle game, it depends on pattern recognition and logical deduction. In a strategy game, it depends on planning — specifically, on making decisions now that will pay off (or backfire) later, often many moves or minutes down the line.

The core loop of a strategy game typically involves three things:

1. **Gathering information** about the current state of the game — your resources, your opponent's position, the terrain, the map.
2. **Making decisions** about how to allocate limited resources — time, money, units, attention — among competing priorities.
3. **Executing and adapting** as the consequences of your decisions (and your opponent's) unfold.

This loop is what makes strategy games feel brainy. You're constantly weighing trade-offs. Build more workers to grow your economy, or train soldiers to defend against an early attack? Expand to a new territory for its resources, or consolidate what you have? Invest in research for long-term advantage, or spend on immediate military strength?

These aren't questions with objectively correct answers. They depend on context — what your opponent is doing, what stage of the game you're in, what risks you're willing to take. That contextual decision-making is the heart of strategic thinking, and it's why people find these games so satisfying (and so time-consuming).

## The Major Subgenres

Strategy games have branched into several distinct subgenres, each emphasizing different aspects of strategic thinking.

### Turn-Based Strategy (TBS)

The oldest form. Players take alternating turns, each having unlimited time (usually) to think, plan, and execute actions. Chess is the prototypical turn-based strategy game — no luck, no hidden information, pure strategic planning.

Modern turn-based strategy games include:

**Civilization** (1991-present): Sid Meier's masterwork. You guide a civilization from the Stone Age to the Space Age, managing cities, research, diplomacy, culture, and military. Each turn represents years or decades. A single game can take 8-12 hours. The series has sold over 60 million copies and is probably the most influential strategy game franchise ever made.

**XCOM** (1994-present): Tactical turn-based combat against alien invaders. You manage a squad of soldiers, each with unique abilities, in tense grid-based battles where a single bad decision can get your best soldier killed permanently. The permadeath mechanic — losing a character means they're gone forever — creates real stakes that most games can't match.

**Fire Emblem** (1990-present): Japanese tactical RPG where you move units on grid maps, manage rock-paper-scissors weapon relationships, and deal with permadeath. The series has been running for over 30 years and influenced an entire generation of tactical games.

The advantage of turn-based design is depth. Without time pressure, players can consider every option, plan several moves ahead, and appreciate the strategic implications of each decision. The disadvantage? Waiting for your opponent's turn, which can be tedious in multiplayer.

### Real-Time Strategy (RTS)

Everything happens simultaneously. You build bases, gather resources, train armies, and fight — all in real time, while your opponent does the same. The RTS genre demands both strategic planning and rapid execution, a combination some find exhilarating and others find overwhelming.

**StarCraft** (1998) and its sequel **StarCraft II** (2010) are the genre's crown jewels. Three asymmetric races — Terran, Protoss, Zerg — with completely different units, buildings, and strategies. Professional StarCraft in South Korea became a legitimate spectator sport, with players earning six-figure salaries and matches broadcast on dedicated TV channels. At the highest level, professional players execute 300-400 actions per minute while maintaining strategic awareness across the entire map.

**Age of Empires** (1997-present) blends historical settings with real-time base building and combat. The series, particularly Age of Empires II, remains actively played decades after release, with a thriving competitive scene.

**Command & Conquer** (1995) popularized the RTS genre alongside Warcraft, introducing full-motion video cutscenes and a campy Cold War narrative that made strategy games feel cinematic.

The RTS genre peaked commercially in the early 2000s and has declined somewhat since, partly because the multitasking demands create a steep learning curve that discourages casual players. But the strategic depth and competitive intensity keep a dedicated community engaged.

### Grand Strategy

Grand strategy games operate at the highest level of abstraction — you control entire nations, empires, or civilizations across decades or centuries. Military combat is just one element alongside diplomacy, economics, politics, religion, and culture.

**Paradox Interactive** dominates this space with a suite of interconnected grand strategy titles:

- **Europa Universalis IV** (1444-1821): Guide any nation through the early modern period. Colonize continents, manage trade empires, fight religious wars, work through dynastic politics.
- **Crusader Kings III** (867-1453): Play as a medieval dynasty, not a nation. Your character has personality traits, skills, and relationships that affect gameplay. Succession crises, scheming relatives, and dynastic marriages are as important as military campaigns.
- **Hearts of Iron IV** (1936-1948): World War II at the strategic level. Manage industrial production, research military technologies, draw battle plans on a detailed map of the world.
- **Stellaris** (far future): Build a space empire, encounter alien civilizations, manage your economy and military across a galaxy of procedurally generated stars.

These games are notoriously complex. Hearts of Iron IV has a learning curve measured in dozens of hours. But for players who invest that time, the depth of simulation and the emergent stories that arise from complex systems interacting are unmatched.

### 4X Games

Named for their four core activities: eXplore, eXpand, eXploit, eXterminate. 4X games start you with almost nothing — a single city, a lone scout — and task you with building an empire through exploration, colonization, resource development, and (usually) conquest.

Civilization is the most famous 4X game, but the subgenre also includes space-based entries like **Stellaris**, **Master of Orion**, and **Endless Space**, as well as fantasy variations like **Endless Legend** and **Age of Wonders**.

The 4X structure creates a natural narrative arc: early exploration and optimism, mid-game expansion and competition, and late-game conflict and domination. This arc mirrors real [historical](/history) patterns of empire-building, which is part of the genre's appeal.

### Tower Defense

A simpler, more focused strategy subgenre. Enemies advance along fixed paths, and you place defensive structures (towers) to destroy them before they reach your base. The strategy lies in choosing which towers to build, where to place them, and when to upgrade.

**Plants vs. Zombies** (2009) made the genre mainstream. **Bloons TD 6** maintains a massive player base. The auto-battler genre (Teamfight Tactics, Dota Auto Chess) draws heavily on tower defense principles.

### Auto-Battlers and New Hybrids

The strategy game space continues to evolve. **Auto-battlers** — where you draft and position units that fight automatically — strip away execution demands and focus purely on strategic decision-making. **Into the Breach** (2018) created a unique subgenre: a turn-based tactics game that shows you exactly what the enemy will do next turn, turning combat into a puzzle about minimizing damage.

## The Psychology of Strategic Thinking

Why do some people play 14-hour Civilization sessions while others bounce off strategy games in minutes? The answer involves cognitive preferences, personality, and what psychologists call "need for cognition."

### Decision Fatigue vs. Decision Pleasure

Strategy games demand constant decision-making. For people high in need for cognition — who genuinely enjoy thinking through problems — this is the appeal. Each decision is a mini-puzzle, and the accumulation of good decisions into a winning strategy is deeply satisfying.

For others, the constant decision load is exhausting. Decision fatigue — the deterioration of decision quality after making many decisions — is a real phenomenon. Strategy games can trigger it quickly in players who don't find the decision-making inherently rewarding.

### The Planning Horizon

Different strategy subgenres appeal to different planning preferences. Some people think naturally in terms of the next few seconds (RTS). Others think in terms of the next few turns (tactical TBS). Still others think in terms of the next century (grand strategy). Your preferred planning horizon — how far into the future you naturally think — predicts which subgenre you'll enjoy.

### Flow States

The best strategy games create flow — that state of deep, focused engagement where time disappears and you're completely absorbed in the activity. Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi identified flow as requiring a balance between challenge and skill, clear goals, and immediate feedback. Good strategy games provide all three: the challenge scales with your skill, the objectives are clear, and you can see the results of your decisions playing out.

The "one more turn" phenomenon in Civilization — where you intend to stop playing but keep going because you want to see what happens next — is a flow state symptom. It's also one reason strategy game sessions tend to be very long.

## Strategy Games and AI

Strategy games have become one of the most important testing grounds for [artificial intelligence](/artificial-intelligence) research.

### Chess: The Original AI Benchmark

IBM's Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov in 1997, marking one of AI's most famous milestones. Deep Blue used brute-force search — evaluating 200 million positions per second — combined with handcrafted evaluation functions. It was impressive engineering but not "intelligence" in any meaningful sense.

Google DeepMind's AlphaZero (2017) changed the game. Starting with nothing but the rules of chess, AlphaZero taught itself to play at superhuman level in just four hours through self-play and reinforcement learning. It developed unconventional strategies that surprised human grandmasters, including a preference for active piece activity over material advantage.

### StarCraft II: The RTS Challenge

DeepMind's AlphaStar (2019) defeated top professional StarCraft II players, a far more complex challenge than chess. StarCraft II involves incomplete information (you can't see your opponent's base), real-time decision-making, thousands of possible actions per step, and strategic reasoning over 20+ minute games. AlphaStar used [deep learning](/deep-learning) with reinforcement learning, training on human replays before improving through self-play.

### Why Strategy Games Matter for AI

Strategy games test capabilities that are directly relevant to real-world AI applications:

- **Planning under uncertainty**: Making decisions with incomplete information
- **Resource allocation**: Distributing limited resources optimally
- **Opponent modeling**: Predicting and adapting to the behavior of others
- **Multi-agent coordination**: Managing many entities toward a common goal
- **Transfer learning**: Adapting strategies learned in one context to new situations

The techniques developed for game AI — Monte Carlo tree search, deep reinforcement learning, multi-agent training — are being applied to logistics, autonomous vehicles, financial trading, and military planning.

## The Cultural Impact of Strategy Games

Strategy games have influenced culture beyond gaming in several ways.

The **Civilization** series has introduced millions of players to historical concepts, political systems, and the names of world leaders they'd never have encountered otherwise. Research suggests players develop more interest in [history](/history) and geography after playing historical strategy games, though the games' simplified models of complex processes (like colonialism or religious conversion) have been criticized for distorting understanding.

**Chess** remains the world's most played strategy game, with an estimated 600 million regular players. The 2020 Netflix series "The Queen's Gambit" triggered a massive surge in chess interest, with Chess.com gaining millions of new accounts.

**Esports** built on strategy games generate significant revenue. The Dota 2 International tournament's prize pool exceeded $40 million in 2021, funded by player purchases. League of Legends and StarCraft II tournaments draw millions of viewers.

And there's a broader cultural effect: strategy games normalize strategic thinking. Players who spend hundreds of hours making resource allocation decisions, evaluating trade-offs, and thinking about second-order consequences may bring those habits to real-world decision-making. The evidence for this transfer is still debated in [cognitive psychology](/cognitive-psychology), but the possibility is tantalizing.

## The Future of Strategy Games

Several trends are shaping the genre's future.

**AI opponents are getting better.** Traditional strategy game AI relied on cheating — giving computer opponents extra resources or information to compensate for poor decision-making. Modern AI techniques, influenced by research in [machine learning](/machine-learning), are producing opponents that play more humanly and adaptively.

**Procedural generation** creates unique maps, scenarios, and challenges each playthrough, increasing replayability. Games like Dwarf Fortress generate entire worlds with geological histories, civilizations, and ecosystems before you even start playing.

**Accessibility** is improving. The complexity that makes strategy games deep also makes them intimidating. Better tutorials, streamlined interfaces, and difficulty options are opening the genre to wider audiences without sacrificing depth for experienced players.

**Multiplayer asynchronous play** — taking your turn whenever you have time, like a chess-by-mail arrangement — suits strategy games perfectly and makes them ideal for mobile platforms.

Strategy games have been with us since ancient board games like Go (over 2,500 years old) and chess (about 1,500 years old). The technology changes. The appeal of out-thinking an opponent — human or artificial — doesn't.
