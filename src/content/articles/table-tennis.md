# What Is Table Tennis?

Table tennis — also called ping-pong — is a sport in which two or four players hit a lightweight ball back and forth across a net on a hard table using small paddles. Games are played to 11 points, and you need to win by 2. Matches are typically best of 5 or 7 games.

It sounds simple. It is emphatically not. At the competitive level, table tennis is one of the fastest sports in the world. The ball can spin at over 9,000 rpm, travel at 70+ mph, and the time between shots is measured in fractions of a second. Professional players make split-second decisions that recreational players can barely follow with their eyes.

## The Basics

The table is 9 feet long, 5 feet wide, and 2.5 feet high, with a 6-inch net across the middle. The ball is 40mm in diameter, weighs 2.7 grams, and is made of plastic (formerly celluloid). Players use paddles covered in rubber — the type of rubber dramatically affects how the ball behaves.

Serving alternates every 2 points. The server must toss the ball at least 6 inches from an open palm, strike it so it bounces once on their side then once on the opponent's side. After the serve, players hit the ball back and forth — it must bounce once on each side. If you miss, hit the net, or hit the ball off the table, your opponent scores.

## The Role of Spin

Spin is what separates casual ping-pong from competitive table tennis. Professional players impart incredible rotation on the ball:

- **Topspin** — The ball dips downward after crossing the net, bouncing forward aggressively. This is the dominant offensive technique.
- **Backspin** — The ball floats and stays low, resisting the opponent's attempts to attack. Used defensively and in serves.
- **Sidespin** — The ball curves laterally. Combined with top or backspin, it creates unpredictable trajectories.

Reading spin — understanding what rotation is on the ball by watching the opponent's paddle angle and stroke — is one of the most critical skills. Get it wrong, and the ball flies off your paddle at bizarre angles.

## The Equipment Arms Race

The rubber covering on table tennis paddles is regulated but still allows for significant variation. "Inverted" rubber (smooth side out) is standard for most players and allows heavy spin. "Pips out" rubber (textured side out) produces less spin but faster, flatter shots. Long-pips rubber actually reverses the opponent's spin — a technique some players use to confuse and frustrate opponents.

The international governing body (ITTF) has repeatedly changed equipment rules to keep the sport accessible and entertaining. They increased the ball size from 38mm to 40mm in 2000, switched from celluloid to plastic balls in 2014, and banned certain types of speed glue — all aimed at slightly slowing the game.

## A Brief History

Table tennis originated in Victorian-era England as an after-dinner parlor game, using improvised equipment — cigar box lids as paddles, champagne corks as balls, and books lined up as nets. Commercially produced sets appeared in the 1880s-1890s.

The sport was formalized with the creation of the International Table Tennis Federation in 1926. It became an [Olympic](/sports-history) sport in 1988. China's dominance has been a defining feature since the 1960s — the country has won 32 of 37 Olympic gold medals in the sport through 2024.

The famous "Ping-Pong [Diplomacy](/diplomacy)" of 1971, when American and Chinese table tennis players exchanged visits, helped thaw relations between the two countries and paved the way for President Nixon's historic visit to China.

## Health Benefits

Table tennis is excellent [exercise](/exercise-[physiology](/physiology)) despite its modest appearance. It improves hand-eye coordination, reflexes, and fine motor skills. It provides moderate cardiovascular conditioning. Research suggests it may be particularly beneficial for brain health — a Japanese study found that table tennis improved cognitive function in elderly participants more than other physical activities.

The sport is also remarkably accessible. You can play at any age, any [fitness](/fitness) level, and in a very small space. Many rehabilitation programs use table tennis for stroke recovery, Parkinson's disease [management](/management), and mobility improvement.

## Why It Captivates

At the recreational level, ping-pong is fun precisely because the barrier to entry is so low — you can have a decent rally within minutes of picking up a paddle. At the competitive level, the speed, spin, strategy, and reflexes required make it genuinely breathtaking to watch.

The gap between casual and professional play is enormous, and bridging it is addictive. Every time you learn to read a new spin, execute a new technique, or win a point you couldn't have won before, you understand a little better why an estimated 300 million people play this sport worldwide.
