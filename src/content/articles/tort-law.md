# What Is Tort Law?

Tort law is the branch of civil law that deals with situations where one person's actions (or failures to act) cause harm to another person. The word "tort" comes from the Latin *tortum*, meaning "wrong" or "twisted." When someone wrongs you — injures you, damages your property, harms your reputation, or invades your privacy — tort law provides the legal framework for seeking compensation.

Unlike criminal law, which is about punishing wrongdoing on behalf of society, tort law is about making the injured party whole. The goal is typically money — enough to compensate for medical bills, lost income, property damage, pain and suffering, and other losses caused by the defendant's conduct.

## The Three Categories

### Intentional Torts

The defendant deliberately caused harm. Common intentional torts include:

- **Battery** — Intentional harmful or offensive physical contact
- **Assault** — Intentionally causing someone to fear imminent harmful contact
- **False imprisonment** — Intentionally confining someone against their will
- **Trespass** — Intentionally entering someone's property without permission
- **Defamation** — Publishing false statements that damage someone's reputation (libel if written, slander if spoken)
- **Intentional infliction of emotional distress** — Outrageous conduct deliberately causing severe emotional harm

### Negligence

By far the most common tort. Negligence occurs when someone fails to exercise the care that a reasonable person would exercise in the same situation, and that failure causes harm. Car accidents, medical malpractice, slip-and-fall cases, and product defects are typical negligence claims.

Four elements must be proven:

1. **Duty** — The defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care (doctors owe patients care; drivers owe other motorists care)
2. **Breach** — The defendant failed to meet that standard of care
3. **Causation** — The breach actually caused the plaintiff's injury (both factual cause and proximate cause)
4. **Damages** — The plaintiff suffered actual harm (physical, financial, or emotional)

### Strict Liability

In certain situations, a defendant is liable regardless of fault or intent. Product manufacturers are strictly liable for defective products that cause injury. Owners of dangerous animals are strictly liable for harm they cause. People who engage in inherently dangerous activities (like blasting or storing hazardous materials) are strictly liable for resulting damage.

The rationale: some activities or products are so inherently risky that the person conducting them should bear responsibility for any resulting harm, even if they took reasonable precautions.

## How Tort Cases Work

Most tort claims proceed as civil [lawsuits](/law). The injured party (plaintiff) files a complaint in court. The defendant responds. Both sides conduct discovery (exchanging evidence and taking depositions). If the case doesn't settle — and roughly 95% of civil cases settle before trial — it goes before a judge or jury.

The standard of proof in tort cases is "preponderance of the evidence" — more likely than not (essentially 51% probability). This is much lower than the "beyond a reasonable doubt" standard in criminal cases.

## Damages

If the plaintiff wins, the court awards damages:

**Compensatory damages** make the plaintiff whole:
- Medical expenses (past and future)
- Lost wages and earning capacity
- Property repair or replacement
- Pain and suffering
- Emotional distress

**Punitive damages** punish the defendant for particularly bad conduct and deter others. They're only available in cases of willful misconduct or gross [negligence](/jurisprudence).

## Why Tort Law Matters

Tort law serves several important functions:

**Compensation** — Injured people receive money to cover their losses. Without tort law, victims of negligence or intentional harm would bear all costs themselves.

**Deterrence** — The threat of liability encourages businesses and individuals to act carefully. Product safety, medical standards, and workplace safety all improve when negligence carries financial consequences.

**Justice** — Tort law provides a peaceful mechanism for resolving disputes about harm. Without it, injured parties might resort to self-help or simply suffer in silence.

## The Tort Reform Debate

Tort law is politically contentious. Reform advocates argue that excessive litigation drives up costs for businesses and consumers, that "frivolous" lawsuits clog the courts, and that large jury awards are unpredictable and sometimes disproportionate. They support caps on damages, limits on attorney fees, and higher standards for filing claims.

Opponents argue that tort reform primarily benefits corporations at the expense of injured individuals, that the "frivolous lawsuit" narrative is exaggerated, and that the threat of liability is the main incentive for companies to maintain safety standards.

The debate continues, with different states adopting different reform measures. Wherever you stand politically, understanding tort law matters — because the principles of duty, care, and responsibility that it embodies affect how virtually every business and institution operates.
